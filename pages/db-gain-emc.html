dB, Gain & EMC/EMI

A ground-up explanation of decibels and gain — from the logarithm identity that makes the math tractable, through every reference unit you'll encounter on a spectrum analyzer, to how CISPR and FCC translate dBµV/m into pass/fail decisions at the test lab.

2026-06-26

↓ start reading

01 Why dB Exists

The dynamic range problem

An oscilloscope displays a few millivolts to tens of volts — a ratio of about 10,000:1. A spectrum analyzer measuring radiated emissions must span from the noise floor (~1 µV/m) to near-field spurs that can exceed 1 V/m — a ratio of 1,000,000:1. Plotting that on a linear Y-axis makes the smaller signals invisible.

The fix is a logarithm. log₁₀(1,000,000) = 6. Now your axis goes from 0 to 6 instead of 0 to 1,000,000. The decibel formalizes this: it is ten times the base-10 log of a power ratio. [1]

There is a second reason engineers reach for dB: cascaded stages multiply in the linear domain but add in the log domain. A preamp (×100) followed by a cable (×0.5) followed by an amplifier (×10) is a multiplication you have to work out by hand. In dB it is +40 − 3 + 20 = +57 dB — five-second arithmetic. [1]

Core Definition

dB is always a ratio between two quantities of the same type. It is never an absolute unit by itself. Add a reference letter (m, V, µV…) and it becomes absolute.

02 The Math

Two formulas — know when to use each

Power Ratio

dB = 10 × log₁₀(Pout / Pin) [2]

Voltage (or Current) Ratio — same impedance on both sides

dB = 20 × log₁₀(Vout / Vin) [2]

The factor changes from 10 to 20 because power is proportional to V². Squaring inside a log pulls the exponent out front: 10 × log₁₀(V²/V₀²) = 10 × 2 × log₁₀(V/V₀) = 20 × log₁₀(V/V₀). This is not a convention — it is an algebraic identity.

Easy mistake — −3 dB means two different things

In the power domain: −3 dB = half the power. In the voltage domain: −3 dB = 70.7% of the amplitude (×1/√2). These are not the same. Confusing them is especially common when working across mixed domains (e.g. converting an antenna's voltage output to a received power figure). [4]

03 Absolute Reference Units

When dB gets a letter suffix

Plain dB is dimensionless — it's a ratio. Anchor the denominator to a fixed reference and you get an absolute level. Every suffix tells you the reference.

The 50 Ω conversion: dBm ↔ dBµV

In any 50 Ω system, power and voltage levels are related by a fixed offset: [6]

50 Ω conversion (memorize this)

dBµV = dBm + 107   |   dBm = dBµV − 107

Where does 107 come from? 1 mW into 50 Ω gives V = √(0.001 × 50) = 0.2236 V = 223,600 µV. Then 20·log₁₀(223,600) = 107.0 dB. [6]

75 Ω exception

The +107 offset is only valid in 50 Ω systems. In a 75 Ω system (cable TV, video), the same power produces 22.5% more voltage — the offset becomes +108.75. Never use +107 when your system impedance is not 50 Ω. [7]

04 Gain & Cascading

Why dB makes link budgets arithmetic

Gain is just the ratio of output to input. [3] A stage that takes 10 mV in and delivers 1 V out has a voltage gain of 100 — or +40 dB. A stage that passes half the power has a gain of −3 dB.

When stages are cascaded, total dB gain is the arithmetic sum: [39]

Cascaded gain example — radiated emissions measurement chain each block adds its dB value to the running total
Total path gain/loss = algebraic sum of all stage values in dB. Adding stages never requires multiplication. These are illustrative example values. In a real measurement: antenna AF varies by frequency and requires the manufacturer's calibration table; cable loss depends on cable type, length, and frequency; preamp gain of +33 dB is from the Com-Power PAM-103 datasheet. [19]
Key finding

Impedance mismatch between cascaded stages can shift the actual gain by ±0.5 dB or more from the nominal. A stage nominally at 17 dB can range from 16.49 dB to 17.54 dB depending on the impedance presented by the preceding stage. In an EMC measurement chain, this error propagates directly into the field-strength reading. [40]

05 Common Mistakes

Four errors that trip up experienced engineers

1 — Using 10× instead of 20× for voltage

A 10:1 voltage ratio correctly gives 20·log₁₀(10) = 20 dB. Applying the power formula gives 10·log₁₀(10) = 10 dB — exactly half the correct answer. In an EMC measurement chain with multiple stages, this error compounds: an antenna factor of 20 dB/m misread as 10 dB/m shifts your field-strength result by 10 dB — easily the difference between pass and fail. [6]

2 — Power vs voltage interpretation of −3 dB

When an op-amp datasheet says "−3 dB bandwidth at 1 MHz," it means the power at 1 MHz is half the passband power. The voltage amplitude is 70.7% of mid-band — not 50%. Engineers mixing power and voltage domains in the same calculation without tracking the factor-of-2 difference in the exponent produce errors of up to 6 dB. [4]

3 — Applying +107 in non-50 Ω systems

The dBm → dBµV offset of +107 is derived from 50 Ω only. Cable television systems operate at 75 Ω; bio-instrumentation front-ends present kiloohm source impedances. At 75 Ω the offset is ~+108.75. At 600 Ω (some audio systems) it is ~+117.8. Always derive the offset for your actual impedance: offset = 20·log₁₀(√(1000R)) where R is in ohms. [7]

4 — Forgetting cable loss in the measurement chain

Cable loss is always positive dB of attenuation (a negative gain). It increases with frequency and with length. Representative values from an online reference (unverified — confirm from manufacturer datasheets): RG-58 at 144 MHz ≈ 6.2 dB per 100 ft; at 1.2 GHz ≈ 21.1 dB per 100 ft. LMR-400 is substantially lower: ≈ 1.5 dB/100 ft at 144 MHz, ≈ 4.8 dB/100 ft at 1.2 GHz. [20] If you omit cable loss from your measurement-chain calculation, your recovered field-strength reading is optimistic by that many dB — meaning you could declare a passing product that is actually failing. [22]

06 EMC/EMI Overview

Two tests, two noise paths

EMC testing splits into emissions (your device radiating/injecting noise outward) and immunity (your device surviving external noise). The FCC Part 15 framework only mandates emissions testing — no FCC immunity requirements exist for most commercial products. [38] IEC/CISPR standards cover both.

07 Conducted Emissions

The LISN and what it measures

Conducted emissions testing places a LISN (Line Impedance Stabilization Network) between the AC mains and your device. The LISN contains a 5 µH inductor that presents a known 50 Ω impedance to the device under test at RF frequencies, while blocking RF from the building's AC wiring. [23] You tap across the 50 Ω terminal and route it to an EMI receiver.

The LISN is not optional — without it, the impedance the device "sees" at its power port is undefined and measurement-to-measurement variation is enormous. The 5 µH inductance and 50 Ω termination together are the source impedance your device's noise is being measured into, so all limit values are calibrated to this exact setup.

Test range: 150 kHz to 30 MHz. CISPR 16 specifies 9 kHz resolution bandwidth, maximum segment span 2.7 MHz, sweep time 100 ms/MHz. [24]

56 dBµV converts to 10^(56/20) µV = 631 µV at the LISN port. That is the absolute maximum noise voltage your device may produce on its power lead at 1 MHz, measured into 50 Ω.

08 Radiated Emissions

The measurement chain and its dB budget

What the receiver reads is not the field strength at the device — it is the voltage at the receiver input after the signal has traveled through an antenna, a cable, and possibly a preamplifier. You have to reconstruct the actual field strength using the measurement-chain formula: [16]

Field-strength recovery formula

E (dBµV/m) = Vrx (dBµV) + AF (dB/m) + Cableloss (dB) − Gpreamp (dB)

Each term accounts for one stage of the measurement path. Getting any term wrong shifts the field-strength result by that many dB.

Radiated emissions measurement chain example at 300 MHz — all values in dB
Recovered E-field = receiver reading + antenna factor + cable loss − preamp gain. Every ±1 dB error in any correction factor shifts the final E-field result by ±1 dB. Values shown are illustrative example values — not specifications. Real measurements require per-frequency antenna calibration tables [15] and measured cable loss at each frequency.

Antenna Factor (AF)

AF is the calibration constant that converts the voltage at the antenna connector into the E-field that was incident on the antenna. Units: dB/m (or equivalently dB re 1/m). [15] It is supplied as a frequency table by the antenna manufacturer and must be applied point-by-point across the sweep. A biconical antenna (30–300 MHz) and a log-periodic (300–1000 MHz) are the standard pair per CISPR 16-1-4. [17]

AF error propagates 1:1 into E-field error. For a half-wavelength dipole reference antenna, AF uncertainty is ≥0.3 dB up to 1 GHz and ≥0.5 dB up to 2 GHz — a 5–15% error in the E-field value. [31]

Preamplifier

A broadband RF preamplifier with 20–30 dB gain is inserted between the antenna and receiver to overcome the noise floor limitations of entry-level spectrum analyzers. [18] The Com-Power PAM-103, for example, covers 1 MHz–1 GHz with 33 dB gain and a noise figure below 3.3 dB. [19] In the recovery formula, preamp gain is subtracted because it artificially elevated what the receiver saw — you must remove that artificial boost to get the true field strength.

09 EMI Receiver vs Spectrum Analyzer

Detector types — why the reading changes with the detector

A spectrum analyzer is a general RF instrument. An EMI receiver is a spectrum analyzer built to CISPR 16-1-1 specifications — with tighter requirements on detector accuracy, resolution bandwidth, and input protection. [25] The most important difference is the available detector modes.

DetectorMeasuresCISPR time constants (0.15–30 MHz)Usage
Peakhighest instantaneous level in RBW bucketinstantaneousfast pre-scan; always ≥ QP ≥ avg
Quasi-peakweighted peak — charges fast, decays slowly1 ms charge, 160 ms decay [27]binding limit detector for most CISPR tests
AverageRMS-averaged power over dwell time—binding limit for broadband/continuous noise
RMStrue RMS power—CISPR 16-1-1 newer addition; not always required

Quasi-peak was developed in 1939 to weight impulsive noise in a way that correlates with its subjective interference severity. [28] It charges fast (so it captures brief pulses) but discharges slowly (so isolated pulses read higher than continuous low-level noise). For a pulsed source at 10 kHz repetition rate, peak and quasi-peak read roughly equal (~100 dBµV) while the RMS-average reads ~89 dBµV — 11 dB lower. [27]

Because quasi-peak scans require ~1 second per frequency point, [26] it is standard practice to run a fast peak scan first and only apply quasi-peak at frequencies that approach the limit. This is not an approximation — a product can pass peak and still fail quasi-peak if the noise is impulsive.

EMI receiver vs spectrum analyzer — key hardware difference

EMI receivers include a preselector: a bank of tunable bandpass filters before the first mixer that attenuates out-of-band signals by more than 60 dB. [29] Spectrum analyzers lack this, making them susceptible to intermodulation from strong out-of-band signals — which can produce false responses on an emissions scan. For pre-compliance, this is manageable; for certification, an accredited EMI receiver is required. CISPR filter bandwidths are also defined at −6 dB, not the −3 dB convention used for spectrum analyzer RBW. [29]

10 Immunity Testing

Blasting the DUT — immunity levels in dB

Immunity testing reverses the measurement chain: you generate a calibrated E-field or conducted RF signal and verify that the DUT keeps operating within spec. All levels are expressed in V/m or dBµV, making dB arithmetic directly applicable.

The 100 dB difference between a conducted emissions limit (56 dBµV max at the power port) and a conducted immunity injection (140 dBµV) is not a contradiction — they measure different things at different impedances with different modulation. Emissions: your device injecting into the mains. Immunity: the mains injecting into your device.

11 Margin & Uncertainty

How close is too close to the limit line

EMC scan plot — anatomy Y = dBµV or dBµV/m, X = frequency (log scale)
The horizontal line is the regulatory limit. Your measured emission trace must stay below it. The vertical gap between your worst peak and the limit is the margin in dB.

Designers target a minimum of 3 dB margin as an absolute floor; 4–6 dB is a realistic production buffer. [34] Why not just pass by 0.1 dB?

Military standards and CISPR's 80/80 rule specify 6 dB safety margin as the design target to accommodate manufacturing variation. [32] However, that 6 dB does not cover measurement equipment errors or environmental interactions — some research suggests 12 dB may be needed for genuinely robust products. [33]

Systematic errors as large as 1.95 dB from 30–200 MHz are reported as unaccounted for in the normalized site attenuation (NSA) method defined in CISPR 16-1-4:2019. [31] [UNVERIFIED — source inaccessible] This is presented as a known gap in the standard, not a measurement setup error — it is intrinsic to the NSA technique.

Pre-compliance vs accredited lab

Pre-compliance testing uses the same measurement equipment and techniques but lacks the procedural rigor and formal documentation. When the equipment is properly calibrated and the setup matches the standard, pre-scan data closely predicts formal compliance results. Where pre-compliance differs from accredited results, it is most commonly traceable to a calibration gap (antenna factor, cable loss, or receiver reference level) — all dB-domain errors. No quantified case study data on typical delta was found in accessible sources. [35]

12 Fixing Emissions

How many dB does a ferrite bead actually give you

When you are 8 dB over the limit at 150 MHz, you need something that gives you ≥8 dB of attenuation at that frequency. Here is what to expect from common fixes — and the traps.

Ferrite bead attenuation

Ferrite beads achieve 20–40 dB of attenuation in the 100–500 MHz range — their optimal zone. [36] Below ~10 MHz they contribute almost nothing (impedance is too low). The catch:

  • DC current degrades them. At 50% of rated DC current, a ferrite bead's impedance at 100 MHz can drop from 100 Ω to 10 Ω — a 20 dB reduction in series impedance, and therefore ~10 dB less attenuation depending on the source/load impedance. [36]
  • Resonance can cause gain. If the ferrite bead resonates with a PCB capacitance below its crossover frequency, the resulting LC circuit can add +10 dB peaking instead of attenuation. [36]
  • The 100 MHz spec is not the whole story. Manufacturers specify impedance at only 100 MHz. Two ferrites with identical 100 MHz specs can have drastically different frequency responses. Verify attenuation at your actual problem frequency from the full impedance curve. [36]

Design rule

Every fix that gives you dB of attenuation in the emission path must be verified on the actual PCB under real DC loading conditions. A ferrite that simulates well at low current can fail to attenuate under full load because its impedance has collapsed. Always measure on the final board with nominal supply current flowing.
