CMRR: From First Principles to ADS1299

Common Mode Rejection Ratio is the single most important specification for EEG amplifiers — and the most misunderstood. This page builds CMRR from zero using circuit analogies, then shows exactly how the ADS1299 achieves −110 dB, what degrades it in practice, and how RLD recovers it.

01 The Problem

Two wires, one very large noise source

EEG measures voltage differences across the scalp. Typical signal amplitudes are 10–100 µV. The problem: a person sitting near a mains outlet couples 50/60 Hz interference onto their body capacitively. That interference can easily reach 1–5 V across the whole body — five orders of magnitude larger than the EEG signal you're trying to measure.

The key insight is where that interference lives: it appears equally on every electrode wire. If electrode A sees +1.5 V of 50 Hz noise and electrode B also sees +1.5 V, the difference between them is 0 V. The EEG signal you care about (say, 50 µV from a neural oscillation) appears as a difference between A and B — not equally on both. This is the fundamental split between two types of signals:

COMMON-MODE SIGNAL (Vcm)

A voltage that appears identically on both input wires simultaneously. Mains interference, EMI, and patient body potential are all common-mode. The amplifier should reject this completely.

DIFFERENTIAL SIGNAL (Vdiff)

A voltage that appears as a difference between the two inputs. EEG, ECG, EMG signals are all differential. The amplifier should amplify this exclusively.

You can also write any pair of input voltages (V+ and V−) in terms of these two components:

V+ = Vcm + Vdiff/2
V− = Vcm − Vdiff/2
→ Vdiff = V+ − V−    Vcm = (V+ + V−) / 2

A perfect differential amplifier would amplify only Vdiff and produce zero output for any Vcm, no matter how large. In reality, no amplifier is perfect — it amplifies both, just at very different gains. CMRR is the ratio of how much it amplifies the wanted signal versus the unwanted one.

Common-Mode vs Differential — Signal Anatomy

Both electrodes pick up the same mains noise (common-mode, red). The EEG signal appears only as a voltage difference between them (differential, green). The amplifier must amplify the green and ignore the red.

02 The Mathematics

What CMRR actually means, in numbers

An amplifier has two gains: a differential gain Ad (how much it amplifies Vdiff) and a common-mode gain Acm (how much, despite its best efforts, it also amplifies Vcm). CMRR is their ratio expressed in decibels: [1]

CMRR (dB) = 20 × log10(Ad / |Acm|)

The table below translates dB into plain numbers to make this concrete. With the ADS1299's −110 dB CMRR at gain = 12:

KEY INSIGHT

A mains-coupled body voltage of 1 V (very typical in a clinical room) with 110 dB CMRR produces 3.2 µV of differential artifact. An EEG alpha wave is 20–100 µV. So even at spec, common-mode interference contributes artifact in the same order as some brain signals. This is why achieving the datasheet spec in a real system matters enormously — and why real-world CMRR often falls short of the spec.

03 The Instrumentation Amplifier

How a 3-op-amp IA achieves high CMRR

A simple op-amp difference amplifier is the building block, but its CMRR is severely limited by discrete resistor tolerances. The instrumentation amplifier (IA) solves this with a two-stage architecture. [2]

Stage 1 — Input buffers with shared gain resistor

Two op-amps (A1 and A2) each buffer one input with extremely high input impedance. They are cross-coupled through a single gain resistor RG. [2]

The key property: both inputs see the same common-mode voltage, so each op-amp's output also shifts by Vcm. The output stage then subtracts them, and Vcm cancels perfectly — regardless of RG. This is why changing RG changes differential gain without touching CMRR. [2]

There is a second benefit: increasing RG (lowering differential gain) slightly worsens CMRR, while decreasing RG (raising gain) improves it. Higher gain amplifies Vdiff more relative to Vcm, so the ratio Ad/Acm improves. [2] This is why the ADS1299's CMRR spec is quoted at gain = 12 — not at unity gain.

Stage 2 — Difference amplifier

A precision difference amplifier with four resistors (R1–R4) performs the final subtraction. CMRR here depends entirely on how well R1/R2 matches R3/R4. Any imbalance means the two inputs get slightly different gain — so some Vcm leaks through as Vdiff. [3]

RESISTOR MATCHING → CMRR FLOOR

The ratio mismatch directly sets the CMRR ceiling. In a discrete design: [4]

Resistor toleranceWorst-case CMRR (difference stage)
1%~40 dB
0.1%~60 dB
0.01%~80 dB
Laser-trimmed (≤0.005%)>100 dB

This is why the ADS1299 integrates the entire IA on-die with laser-trimmed resistors. No discrete design can match this level of tolerance at affordable cost. [5]

3-Op-Amp IA TopologyConceptual — not to scale

Stage 1: A1 and A2 buffer the inputs; RG sets differential gain without disturbing CMRR. Stage 2: the difference amplifier subtracts, with CMRR determined by resistor matching. The ADS1299 integrates both stages on a single silicon die with laser-trimmed resistors.

The ADS1299 datasheet does not publish a CMRR vs frequency curve. The −110 dB figure is valid at 50 Hz and 60 Hz specifically, under balanced source impedance (no electrode mismatch). 05 Frequency Dependence

Why CMRR degrades with frequency

Every op-amp has a dominant pole — the frequency above which its open-loop gain starts rolling off at 20 dB/decade. Since CMRR depends on differential gain Ad, and Ad rolls off above this pole, CMRR also rolls off. Simultaneously, parasitic capacitances in the resistor network create additional paths for common-mode signals, raising Acm. Both effects compound. [9]

At low frequency: CMRR ≈ Ad,0 / Acm,0 → maximum, 110 dB
Above dominant pole: Ad rolls off at 20 dB/decade
CMRR also rolls off at 20 dB/decade   → −20 dB per decade of frequency increase

The practical consequence: a "90 dB CMRR" spec quoted without a frequency is meaningless. Always check the test frequency. For EEG, the critical frequencies are:

FrequencySourceCMRR importance
0.1–1 HzDC drift, electrode polarizationCMRR less critical (drift is slow, filtered out)
50 / 60 HzMains (capacitive body coupling)Critical — dominant interference source
100 / 120 HzMains harmonicsSecondary concern
1–100 kHzRF switching noise, motorsLow-pass filter before ADC handles this

CMRR peaks at low frequency (DC spec). Above the dominant pole, it falls at 20 dB/decade. The ADS1299 spec is measured at 50/60 Hz — the precise point where mains interference is worst. TI does not publish CMRR at other frequencies.

06 The Dominant CMRR Killer

Electrode impedance imbalance: why your 110 dB amplifier only gives you 60 dB

Here is the core paradox of EEG system design: even if you had a theoretically perfect amplifier with infinite CMRR, a common-mode signal would still appear at its output if the electrode impedances are unbalanced. The reason is pure voltage-divider physics. [10]

How imbalance converts common-mode to differential

Imagine the two electrodes have impedances ZE+ and ZE−, and the amplifier has input impedances Zin+ and Zin− to ground. Each electrode and its corresponding amplifier input forms a voltage divider. If ZE+ ≠ ZE−, the two voltage dividers have different ratios, so the same Vcm arrives at the two amplifier inputs at different amplitudes. This looks like a differential signal. [10]

V_cm (50 Hz noise, e.g. 1 V)
→
Z_E+ ≠ Z_E−
→
V+ ≠ V− at amp inputs
→
Appears as V_diff = ΔZ_E × V_cm / Z_in
→
Amplified as if it were EEG

The degradation in CMRR from this effect is: [10]

CMRReffective ≈ CMRRamp − 20 × log10(ΔZE / Zin)
where ΔZE = |Z_E+ − Z_E−| and Z_in = amplifier input impedance

Let's put real numbers through this formula with the ADS1299 (Zin = 2 GΩ differential = 1 GΩ per side, CMRRamp = 110 dB): [8]

100 Ω (tight gel electrodes, freshly applied)−140 dB110 dB (amp-limited)Effectively limited by amp
1 kΩ (typical wet gel)−120 dB110 dB (amp-limited)Still spec-limited

REAL-WORLD MEASUREMENT

A TI E2E forum report documented that a complete ADS1299 front-end measured only ~60 dB CMRR in practice, despite the datasheet spec of −110 dB. [12] This is consistent with the math above: 50 kΩ electrode imbalance (common in clinical EEG) degrades system CMRR to ~86 dB before any other losses.

Filter capacitors make it worse

Every EEG front-end includes small ESD protection capacitors on the input pins. These create a parallel impedance path whose AC impedance (XC = 1 / 2πfC) is frequency-dependent. At 50 Hz: [13]

C = 100 pF → XC = 1/(2π × 50 × 100×10⁻¹²) ≈ 32 MΩ at 50 Hz
C = 1 nF   → XC ≈ 3.2 MΩ at 50 Hz
C = 10 nF  → XC ≈ 320 kΩ at 50 Hz   ← same order as electrode impedance → severe CMRR hit

If the two ESD capacitors are not matched to tight tolerance, they form an asymmetric impedance divider in addition to the electrode mismatch. The mitigation: use capacitors no larger than 100 pF with ≤1% tolerance. [13]

Voltage-Divider CMRR DegradationElectrode impedance imbalance → differential artifact

Same common-mode voltage (Vcm) arrives at both electrodes. Unequal electrode impedances (ZE+ ≠ ZE−) form unequal voltage dividers with the amplifier input impedance (Zin). Each input sees a slightly different fraction of Vcm. The difference is indistinguishable from an EEG signal.

KEY FINDING

The amplifier's input impedance is the key defense. With Zin = 2 GΩ (ADS1299), a 50 kΩ electrode imbalance only causes 0.0025% voltage division. This is why high input impedance is not optional for EEG — it directly sets the system CMRR ceiling regardless of how good the amplifier's intrinsic CMRR is. [11]

07 Right Leg Drive

Active CMRR recovery: driving the body with its own noise

Right Leg Drive (RLD) is the most effective CMRR improvement technique for EEG. The name comes from ECG, where an electrode on the right leg was historically used. In EEG it is typically a forehead or earlobe electrode, but the principle is the same.

How RLD works

The common-mode voltage (Vcm) on the patient's body is sensed by averaging the PGA outputs. This average is inverted and fed back to the patient via a dedicated electrode. The result: the body's own common-mode voltage is actively cancelled by driving it with a signal of equal amplitude and opposite phase. [14]

Sense V_cm from PGA outputs (average)
→
Invert and amplify
→
Feed back to patient body via BIAS electrode
→
Body V_cm reduced by loop gain A

The CMRR improvement from the RLD loop is: [14]

CMRR improvement = (1 + A)
where A = closed-loop gain of the RLD feedback loop

CMRRsystem ≈ CMRRamp × (1 + A)

More channels connected to the RLD sense input increases A (more averaging = stronger sense signal), improving CMRR at DC and 50/60 Hz. [15]

RLD bandwidth and the RC network

The RLD loop includes an RC feedback network for stability. Its bandwidth determines how well RLD works as a function of frequency: [16]

fBW = 1 / (2π × Rf × Cf)

Rf = 1 MΩ, Cf = 1.5 nF → fBW ≈ 106 Hz   (datasheet recommendation)
Rf = 392 kΩ, Cf = 10 nF → fBW ≈ 40 Hz    (ADS1299EEG-FE board — more conservative)

KEY FINDING

One published design achieved 143 dB measured CMRR with the ADS1299 by configuring BIAS as an open-loop amplifier (no RC feedback network) with a 1 MΩ series resistor on BIAS_OUT for current limiting. This eliminated the need for a 50/60 Hz notch filter entirely. [15]

WARNING — RLD STABILITY

The RLD loop is a feedback amplifier with phase-sensitive stability. At frequencies where the loop gain reaches 0 dB before the phase recovers from −180°, the system will oscillate. Driving the cable and body capacitance introduces phase lag. The RC network provides a zero to increase phase margin, but also reduces loop gain above the bandwidth. If you extend the bandwidth too aggressively to improve high-frequency CMRR, stability margin shrinks. Always verify phase margin before removing or reducing the RC network. The mandatory 1 MΩ resistor on BIAS_OUT also limits current for patient safety per IEC 60601.

EEG signals are ~10 µV. At this amplitude, even a tiny residual common-mode interference overwhelms the recording. RLD is not optional for EEG — it is mandatory. [15]

08 Driven Shield

BIAS_SHD: eliminating cable-to-shield capacitive pickup

The RLD circuit reduces the patient's body common-mode voltage. A separate but complementary technique — the driven shield — addresses a different problem: capacitive coupling between the signal wire and its surrounding cable shield.

A passively-grounded cable shield has a fixed potential (GND). The signal wire inside it carries the patient signal plus common-mode noise, creating a time-varying voltage difference between wire and shield. This charges the parasitic capacitance between wire and shield, loading the signal and introducing asymmetric noise coupling.

A driven shield (also called active shield or bootstrapped shield) drives the shield conductor to track the signal wire potential in real time. The wire-to-shield voltage is then always nearly zero — no charging current flows, cable capacitance effectively disappears. [17]

On the ADS1299EEG-FE evaluation board, the BIAS_SHD pin provides a buffered output for exactly this purpose. The outer shields of electrode cables are connected to this signal. However, the shield drive components are not factory-installed — jumper JP17 (pins 1–2) must be shorted to enable it. [17]

PRACTICAL LIMITATION

The ADS1299 datasheet does not specify the output bandwidth, phase margin, or slew rate of BIAS_SHD as a separate circuit element. At frequencies where the shield drive introduces phase lag (the driven signal lags the wire signal), the shield voltage difference becomes nonzero again — and at high enough frequencies, an out-of-phase shield can increase rather than decrease coupling. The safe operating frequency range for BIAS_SHD is not published by Texas Instruments. [NEEDS RESEARCH: ADS1299 BIAS_SHD bandwidth specification]

09 System CMRR

Putting it all together: what you actually measure

The datasheet CMRR is the amplifier's intrinsic rejection, measured in a lab with balanced, low-impedance sources. Real EEG introduces several degradation mechanisms that cascade: [18]

IEC 60601 requirements for EEG

IEC 60601-2-26 is the safety standard for EEG equipment. Industry sources cite its minimum CMRR requirement as −89 dB, tested with a 2 Vrms common-mode signal at mains frequency. Note: the primary source for this claim could not be independently fetched during research — see reference [19]. [19]

KEY FINDING

IEC 60601-2-26 requires only −89 dB CMRR for EEG devices. The ADS1299 spec of −110 dB provides 21 dB of margin above this floor — but only under ideal conditions. With typical electrode impedance imbalance, system CMRR can fall to 60–80 dB, potentially below the IEC floor. This is why electrode preparation and impedance checking are not optional clinical steps — they directly determine whether the device meets its safety standard in use.
