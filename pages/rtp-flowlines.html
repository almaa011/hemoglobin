Section 01

Classification & Core Mechanics: Bonded vs. Unbonded RTP

The term "RTP" is frequently used loosely to cover two structurally distinct product families that behave very differently under combined loading. The split is: Unbonded RTP (also called Flexible Composite Pipe, FCP) and Bonded RTP (also called Thermoplastic Composite Pipe, TCP). The governing standards follow the same split — API 17J covers unbonded assemblies; API 17K covers bonded assemblies.[8][9]

1.1 Structural Architecture

Unbonded RTP (FCP)
  • Three basic layers: thermoplastic liner → helically wrapped continuous-fiber reinforcement → outer thermoplastic jacket.[2]
  • Defining characteristic: no mutual adhesive bonding between layers. API Spec 17J requires that at minimum two layers (inclusive of reinforcing and plastic layers) have no adhesive attachment.[8]
  • Layers are free to slide relative to each other under load.
  • Reinforcement is typically aramid fiber tape, glass fiber tape, or steel wire helically wound at ±54.7° (GEK — neutral angle from composite netting theory; hoop:axial stress ratio = 2:1) for pure pressure resistance, with angle adjustments for combined pressure + axial tension service.
  • Typical spool lengths: 300–400 m per reel.[10][6] ⚠ CONFLICT Sources differ: one states 300 m max [10], another states up to 400 m [6]. Likely diameter-dependent.
Bonded RTP / TCP
  • Three integrated layers: inner liner (HDPE, PA12, PVDF, or PEEK) → composite reinforcement with helically wound glass or carbon fiber tapes → outer protective jacket.[3]
  • Layers are fully consolidated: automated tape winding + laser heating + continuous consolidation fuses all layers into a single laminate.[3]
  • Governed by DNV-ST-F119, which classifies TCP as flexible bonded structures with fibre-reinforced thermoplastic matrix laminates produced by tape winding or filament winding.[7]
  • No autoclave required; continuous production achieves spoolable lengths up to 6 km.[3]
  • Reinforcement fiber volume: approximately 33–40% in glass fiber-reinforced PE matrix.[4b]
Cross-section schematic — Unbonded RTP vs. Bonded TCP

Layer interaction under combined internal pressure + axial tension

In unbonded RTP, the reinforcement layer bears hoop stress from internal pressure while the liner handles chemical isolation. Under axial tension (e.g., gravity load on a sloping line, drag during installation), the liner and jacket cannot transfer load efficiently through the reinforcement — each layer responds independently. This means the end-fittings carry disproportionate axial load, making end-fitting design the critical load path for tensile resistance.[14]

In bonded TCP, consolidated layers form a single anisotropic composite cylinder. Under combined loading, the tape-wound reinforcement plies at helical angles carry both hoop and axial components simultaneously — analogous to a biaxially stressed composite pressure vessel. The bonded interface transfers interlaminar shear, which is a delamination initiation site under cyclic loading. Polymer-reinforcement pairing directly sets the pipe's pressure/temperature envelope. Over 60% of RTP failures in service have been attributed to incorrect material selection in the reinforcement layer.[5]

Operating envelope warning

Field pressures in deepwater riser and high-pressure flowline service can reach 600 barg and 200°C (recognized upper bounds for offshore flexible pipe systems — general engineering knowledge; confirm against specific project operating envelope) — far exceeding the current qualification envelope of commercially available RTP and TCP. RTP is not a direct steel replacement for high-pressure sour gas service; it occupies a specific operating window bounded by liner polymer properties, fiber thermal stability, and end-fitting qualification.

1.3 End-Fitting Design & Field Termination

End-fittings are the most mechanically critical element in any RTP system — the only structural interface between the proprietary composite pipe and field infrastructure. The fitting design differs fundamentally between unbonded and bonded systems.

Unbonded RTP — Swaged / Crimped Mechanical Fittings

  • Installation process (2-step): Step 1 — insert steel mandrel into the liner bore. Step 2 — crimp/swage outer sleeve onto the pipe OD using portable hydraulic tooling.[12] No heat required; tooling is field-portable.
  • Single swaged system: non-metallic lining insert keeps fluid away from steel, creating a corrosion-free mechanical connection. Rated for high-pressure service (125–150 bar / 1,813–2,175 psi).[22]
  • Double swaged system: heavy-duty variant for pressures above 150 bar (2,175 psi), high axial loads, and dynamic (cyclic) service.[29]
  • Body material: typically carbon steel with corrosion-resistant plating or 316L/duplex stainless steel for aggressive chemical environments. (General engineering knowledge — confirm with fitting vendor for specific project service.)
  • Annulus vent port: every end-fitting includes a vent hole connecting to the annulus (space between liner, reinforcement, and outer jacket). This allows permeated gases to vent freely rather than accumulate.[12]

Bonded TCP — Electrofusion / Melt-Fused Fittings

  • Electrofusion process: Insert pipe end into fitting socket → apply prescribed voltage (typically 40 V) → embedded heater coils melt both the fitting interior and the pipe exterior → polymer flows together, producing a homogeneous fusion weld → cool for specified hold time.[28]
  • Qualified installation temperature range: typically 14°F minimum to 113°F maximum (-10°C to +45°C).[30] Cold-weather installations require pre-heating the joint zone.
  • Result: a melt-fused joint with no mechanical grip — the joint strength depends on thermoplastic weld integrity, making quality control of the fusion cycle (time, voltage, cooling) critical.
  • No annulus gap: bonded TCP has no interstitial annulus, so no vent port is needed and no permeated gas accumulation occurs.

Section 02

Industry-Specific Operational Challenges

2.1 Unbonded RTP — Failure Modes

Rapid Gas Decompression (RGD)

RGD is the most structurally destructive failure mode unique to polymer-lined systems. The mechanism is directly analogous to cavitation in fluids — but happening inside a solid.

  • Physics: High-pressure gas (CO₂, H₂S, CH₄) dissolves into and saturates the liner polymer matrix under operating conditions. During an emergency shutdown or blowdown, system pressure drops faster than gas can diffuse back out. The dissolved gas becomes supersaturated, nucleates at microscopic defect sites, and expands violently as internal bubbles — physically rupturing the polymer from the inside out.[20]
  • Risk threshold: Substantial above 50 bar (725 psi). Decompression rates exceeding 10 bar/min significantly amplify damage probability.[18]
  • ISO 23936-2 test rate: 20–40 bar/min at 150 bar and 100°C; Shell MESC 85/301 tests at 40 bar/min at 150°C.[19] ⚠ CONFLICT One study finds that standard ISO 23936-2 acceptance criteria are "not suitable for actual seal pressure validation"[20] — indicating the qualification test may not capture all real-world failure conditions.
  • Outer sheath blistering: Damage manifests as external blistering of the outer HDPE jacket caused by gas that has migrated through the reinforcement annulus reaching the outer sheath.[21]
RGD standard gap

NACE TM0192 tests at 5.2 MPa room temperature for 24 hours with >99% CO₂. NACE TM0297 uses 7–38 MPa at 50–230°C.[21] These produce different failure mechanisms from ISO 23936-2 at 20–40 bar/min, and no unified field guidance exists on which standard best predicts service life for a given operating profile. For sour gas service above 100 bar, specify which test regime applies in the purchase specification.

Interstitial Gas Accumulation & Annulus Permeation

This is a chronic, slow-building failure mode — not an instantaneous event — driven by the thermodynamic reality that no polymer liner achieves zero permeability.

  • CO₂, CH₄, H₂S, and H₂O permeate through the inner liner and travel axially along the annulus toward end-fitting connectors.[10]
  • Gas diffusivity order through typical liner polymers: CH₄ > CO₂ > H₂S. Both diffusion rates and permeability increase with temperature and pressure.[23]
  • PPS liner (one of the better barrier materials): CO₂ permeability measured at 8.6×10⁻⁹ cm³(STP)·cm⁻¹·s⁻¹·bar⁻¹ at 100°C, 150 barg. H₂S permeation through PPS was below GC detection limit (2 ppm) at the same conditions.[25]
  • Consequence for steel-wire unbonded RTP: After CO₂ and H₂S permeate into the annulus and condense water forms, a corrosive solution attacks the steel reinforcement wires.[23] Hydrogen uptake in the carbon steel wires during CO₂-H₂S corrosion creates hydrogen embrittlement risk.[24]
Annulus gas permeation pathway — unbonded RTP

End-Fitting Pull-Out (Thermal Cycling)

Thermal cycling is a documented failure initiation mechanism for swaged end-fittings, driven by differential thermal expansion and polymer aging:

  • During heating cycles: plasticizer loss in the barrier polymer causes permanent shrinkage.
  • During cooling cycles: axial thermal contraction of the liner further reduces liner OD inside the fitting.
  • Combined effect: inner seal ring pressure drops below the threshold needed to retain the liner and carcass inside the fitting body — allowing progressive slip-out and eventual leakage.[26]
  • This failure mode is insidious because it develops incrementally and may not trigger a leak until cumulative damage passes a threshold. [NEEDS RESEARCH: minimum cycle count to end-fitting pull-out failure for steel-wire unbonded RTP in alternating production service]

Lateral Thermal Buckling ("Snaking")

Surface-laid and near-surface-buried RTP lines in high-ambient-temperature environments are susceptible to lateral buckling (also called "snaking") — the pipe displaces laterally from its as-laid position under compressive axial force generated by thermal expansion.

  • Unlike steel pipe where yielding initiates a localized buckle, RTP snaking manifests as a distributed sinusoidal lateral displacement constrained only by soil friction and any berm material placed above the pipe.
  • Governing physics: the compressive end force exceeds the effective axial resistance (pipe weight × soil friction coefficient × embedment). RTP's low weight-per-meter (compared to steel) reduces the restoring force, making snaking more likely at equivalent pressures and temperature differentials.

2.2 Bonded TCP — Failure Modes

Interlayer Delamination under Cyclic Loading

The bonded laminate structure of TCP creates interlaminar shear stress at the fiber-matrix interface under cyclic pressure fluctuations and bending reversals (e.g., pig passes, production ramp-up/down cycles, slug flow).

  • Delamination initiates at manufacturing defects (voids, dry fiber zones, mis-consolidated tape overlaps) and propagates under fatigue loading.
  • Unlike unbonded RTP where layer slip dissipates energy, bonded TCP accumulates interlaminar damage — the laminate stores elastic energy that drives crack growth.
  • Quantified delamination stress thresholds and cycle counts for TCP under realistic combined pressure+bending profiles are [NEEDS RESEARCH — not found in publicly accessible sources during this research round]

Bending Stiffness during Spooling / Tight-Radius Installation

  • The consolidated TCP laminate is inherently stiffer in bending than an equivalent-diameter unbonded RTP because the bonded layers act in composite bending rather than sliding independently.
  • Minimum bend radius for spooling is more restrictive for TCP than for unbonded RTP at equal OD and pressure rating. This constrains reel diameter and limits the maximum continuous pipe length that can be spooled at a given drum size.
  • TCP minimum bend radius vs. unbonded RTP comparison at matched OD/PN: [NEEDS RESEARCH — specific comparative data not found]

Field Repair Complexity

Electrofusion joints in TCP require controlled voltage-time profiles and depend on surface preparation quality. A failed joint cannot be cut back and re-fused indefinitely — each repair shortens the pipe spool. Field repairs in hostile ambient temperatures (below 14°F or above 113°F) fall outside the qualified electrofusion installation window[22] and require tenting, heating, or abandonment of the joint, all of which add cost and schedule risk.

Mechanical Limitations Summary

Key mechanical limits — reference values from testing (DN 150, PN 2.5 MPa pipe)

Critical axial tensile strain: 3%. Minimum respooling bend radius: 1,100 mm (buckling failure with irreversible inner liner yield below this). Maximum safe torsion angle: 58.77° before fiber fracture initiates.[14] These values are test-specific and must be re-verified for other OD/PN combinations — do not extrapolate directly to other pipe classes without vendor confirmation.

Regulatory constraint — US market

RTP is not automatically permitted under ASME B31.4 or B31.8. PHMSA requires an individual Special Use Permit with 6–12 months processing time.[1] Additionally, classical piping stress codes (B31.3, B31.4, B31.8) are inapplicable to RTP — the nonlinear stress-strain behavior with significant hysteresis makes standard FEA pipe libraries produce meaningless results.[1] Vendor-supplied analysis tools are required.

Section 04

Current & Next-Generation Field Mitigations

4.1 Geometrical & Civil Mitigations for Lateral Buckling

For surface-laid or shallowly buried RTP flowlines exposed to temperature differentials, two civil mitigation strategies are employed to prevent uncontrolled lateral buckling:

Snake-Laying (Pre-Engineered Sinusoidal Curves)

  • Principle: Rather than laying the pipe in a straight line, the pipe is intentionally laid in pre-engineered sinusoidal curves along the route corridor. The lateral displacement provides a controlled geometric "slack" that absorbs thermal expansion before the compressive force reaches the buckling trigger load.
  • Advantage: No additional soil material required; effective immediately post-installation; the pipe's natural flexibility helps distribute curvature over a long arc length rather than concentrating it at a single buckle.
  • Constraint: Route corridor must be wide enough to accommodate the sinusoidal layout; survey, design, and GPS guidance required to hit the planned curve geometry during installation. RTP minimum bend radius during lay (20–40× OD[15]) must not be exceeded at any sinusoidal inflection point.
  • Specific design parameters (sinusoidal wavelength, amplitude, required soil friction coefficient) for RTP snake-laying: [NEEDS RESEARCH — these parameters are documented for carbon steel pipelines in published buckling literature but RTP-specific design guidance was not found in accessible sources]

Windrowing / Soil Berming

  • Principle: After laying the pipe, gravel, crushed rock, or native soil is mounded over and alongside the pipe ("windrowing") to increase the lateral soil resistance force. A higher lateral restraint force means a higher trigger load is required before global buckling initiates.
  • Advantage: Effective in straight-lay configurations where route corridor geometry prevents snake-laying. The berm can be sized to fully suppress buckling if enough lateral soil resistance is generated.
  • Limitation: Adds material and labor cost; must be designed with correct berm geometry to mobilize resistance before pipe displaces; berm compaction and long-term erosion resistance must be maintained over the design life.
  • RTP-specific consideration: Because unbonded RTP weighs significantly less per meter than equivalent-diameter CS, the self-weight contribution to vertical stability is lower. This means a taller or wider berm may be required for RTP at the same temperature differential and pressure compared to CS.
Snake-lay vs. windrow — engineering decision

Snake-laying is preferred where the route corridor permits, because it converts the thermal force into a manageable geometric displacement rather than trying to restrain it with soil mass. Windrowing is a secondary tool or where route width constraints preclude sinusoidal geometry. The two are often used together: intentional slack via snake-lay plus light berming to prevent inadvertent over-displacement between inflection points.

4.2 Permeation Management — Annulus Venting Systems

For unbonded RTP in gas-bearing service, the annulus venting system is the primary mitigation for both RGD risk and steel wire corrosion:

  • Vent ports at every end-fitting: API SPEC 15S-qualified fittings include a vent hole connecting the annulus to atmosphere (or to a collection header). Permeated gas is released continuously rather than accumulating to a pressure that could trigger RGD or corrode steel reinforcement.[12]
  • Vent routing options: (a) open vent to atmosphere where H₂S content is negligible; (b) piped to a vapor recovery unit (VRU) for sour gas service; (c) piped back to low-pressure suction for re-injection. For H₂S-bearing service, open venting is not permissible — the vent gas must be treated or routed to a closed system.
  • Controlled decompression during shutdown: The gas pressure in the system should be reduced below the RGD threshold (50 bar[18]) at a decompression rate below the damage threshold (below 10 bar/min[18]) before emergency shutdown valves are fully closed. This requires ESD logic review and HIPPS (High-Integrity Pressure Protection System) valve timing to ensure slow initial blowdown rather than instantaneous closure.

4.3 Advanced & Next-Generation Mitigations

Fiber-Optic Distributed Sensing (DTS / DSS) in the Reinforcement Layer

The helical reinforcement layer of both unbonded RTP and bonded TCP is geometrically suited to accommodate a thin fiber-optic sensing element co-wound with the structural fiber tapes. This enables:

  • Distributed Temperature Sensing (DTS): Detects anomalous temperature profiles along the full pipe length in real-time — early warning of flow assurance events (wax deposition, hydrate formation, blockages), leaks (temperature drop at a leak point from Joule-Thomson expansion), and potential hot-spot degradation zones.
  • Distributed Strain Sensing (DSS): Detects global deformation events — lateral buckling onset, unexpected ground movement (subsidence over weakened zones), and unusual bending strain concentrations at end-fittings or pipe crossings.
  • Integration benefit: Unlike ILI-based tools (which require internal access and periodic runs), a co-wound fiber-optic system provides continuous monitoring without any pigging infrastructure — directly addressing one of RTP's inherent ILI limitations.
  • This approach is an emerging/proposed integration. No specific field deployment data was found in available sources for co-wound fiber in RTP. [NEEDS RESEARCH: commercial implementations of embedded DTS/DSS in RTP or TCP]

Advanced NDT for Field-Joint Validation (Phased Array + TFM)

Field-joint integrity in unbonded RTP (crimped/swaged fittings) is currently validated primarily by visual inspection and witness-point hydraulic test. For high-pressure sour service applications, more rigorous NDE methods are emerging:

  • Phased Array Ultrasonic Testing (PAUT) adapted for polymer: Standard PAUT is calibrated for steel; adapted low-frequency array probes can image disbondment at the liner-reinforcement interface and detect incomplete swage engagement in end-fittings.
  • Total Focusing Method (TFM): Post-processing of full-matrix capture (FMC) data to generate high-resolution images of the fitting-pipe interface — capable of detecting sub-millimeter gaps between mandrel and liner in swaged fittings without disassembly.
  • X-ray CT for laboratory QA: Destructive or CT-scan based verification of fiber architecture quality (voids, delamination, fiber misalignment) in bonded TCP production sections — already used by TCP manufacturers but not yet standard for field-joint inspection.

Thermoplastic Matrix Nanocomposite Liners

Incorporation of nano-clay platelets or graphene oxide sheets into the HDPE or PA12 liner matrix offers a potential path to dramatically reduced gas permeability without changing pipe OD or wall thickness:

  • Nano-clay platelets orient parallel to the pipe wall, creating a tortuous diffusion path for small gas molecules (CO₂, H₂S, CH₄) — extending diffusion path length by a factor proportional to platelet aspect ratio.
  • Potential to reduce CO₂ permeability by 1–2 orders of magnitude compared to unfilled HDPE, potentially eliminating the need for active annulus venting in many moderate-sour applications.
  • Current status: laboratory demonstration stage; not yet commercially deployed in RTP/TCP liner at production scale. [NEEDS RESEARCH: commercial availability and qualification status of nanocomposite RTP liners]
