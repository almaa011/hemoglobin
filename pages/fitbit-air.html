02 Device Overview

Physical Specifications

The Fitbit Air is a $99 screenless fitness tracker, described by Google as "our smallest tracker yet." At 5.2 grams without a band and 8.3 mm thick, it targets 24/7 wrist wear without the bulk of a smartwatch. [1] It is positioned against continuous-wear subscription bands such as WHOOP rather than against display-equipped trackers.

Housing material is recycled polycarbonate and PBT (polybutylene terephthalate). [3] No GPS or barometer are present — route tracking requires a paired phone. [3] The device carries FDA clearance for background Afib detection. [3]

Hardware platform: Fitbit Charge 6, minus two sensors

The Fitbit Air's sensor hardware is derived from the Fitbit Charge 6. DC Rainmaker describes it as "essentially taking a Fitbit Charge 6, and removing the display." [3] Three independent reviewers confirm the same sensor lineage. [3] [10] The two sensors from the Charge 6 that do not carry over:

  • ECG (single-lead electrocardiogram) — present on Charge 6 for on-demand Afib snapshots; absent on Air, which uses continuous background Afib detection via PPG instead
  • EDA (electrodermal activity) — present on Charge 6 for skin conductance / stress measurement; absent on Air

Everything else from the Charge 6 sensor suite is retained. The algorithms running on that hardware are, per a direct statement from Fitbit to DC Rainmaker, "built atop the Pixel Watch 4 algorithms" — Google's own description of the update from the Charge 6 baseline. [3]

KEY FINDING

Charge 6 sensor hardware + Pixel Watch 4-derived algorithms. This explains why the feature set is largely identical to the Charge 6 (same sensor suite, same optical topology) while Google claims slightly better measurement accuracy than the prior generation. Sensor IC part numbers for the Charge 6 are also not publicly confirmed from teardowns, so this lineage helps frame the search but does not resolve the unknown BOM.

03 PPG & Heart Rate Sensor

Optical Heart Rate — Architecture and Sampling

The Fitbit Air uses photoplethysmography (PPG): green LEDs on the underside of the tracker flash at the skin surface, and photodiodes measure how much light bounces back. Each heartbeat pushes a bolus of blood into the wrist capillaries, momentarily increasing light absorption and dropping reflectance. The photodiodes track that modulation and the firmware extracts heart rate. [5]

KEY FINDING

No IC manufacturer or part number for the Fitbit Air PPG module is publicly known. The wavelength figures below come from a Google PPG design patent (US11051706B1) [7] — which describes Google's general PPG architecture, not the specific die inside this device. Without a teardown or FCC internal photo, actual LED wavelengths cannot be confirmed.

PATENT ≠ CONFIRMED BOM

US11051706B1 defines Google's design space — what wavelengths the architecture can accommodate, not what is soldered into the Fitbit Air. The time-multiplexed LED activation scheme described in the patent (each channel fires on a distinct schedule via internal clock; photodetectors placed 1–10 mm from LED sources) [7] is likely representative of the general approach, but no teardown has confirmed part numbers or exact wavelengths for this device.

Sampling rate: what "2-second interval" actually means

Google's published spec cites a "2-second interval" for heart rate. This is not the optical sampling rate. The photodiodes sample at approximately 25 Hz (25 readings per second). The "2-second" figure is the cadence at which processed HR estimates are written to onboard storage. [4] The gap between raw optical sampling and what gets stored is governed by a confidence-gating algorithm.

CONFLICT — MARKETED SPEC vs MEASURED BEHAVIOR

Marketed: 2-second data storage interval.
Measured (wrist, sport activity): Storage interval stretches to approximately 5 seconds because the firmware only writes a data point when the optical signal meets an internal quality threshold. In practice this produces roughly one-fifth the data density of competing devices: 600–1,200 paired data points per analyzed session versus 5,500–5,600 from trackers running 1-second intervals. [4]
Implication: Accuracy statistics published by Google describe only the data points the device chose to keep — not the complete distribution of signal quality across a session.

PPG Data Pipeline — from photons to stored HR schematic only — not confirmed internal block diagram

The ~25 Hz raw optical stream is processed on-chip; only frames passing the confidence gate reach storage. The "2-second spec" describes the nominal gate output rate, not the optical sample rate. During vigorous wrist-worn exercise the gate opens less frequently — stretching to ~5 s.

Accuracy and light leakage

The thin-band form factor creates a hardware accuracy constraint. An 8.3 mm thick tracker with a narrow cuff leaves the optical sensor aperture exposed to ambient light at the edges. In certain workouts, DC Rainmaker measured errors of 20–40 bpm above actual heart rate. [8] This is an optical isolation problem inherent to the device geometry — not a firmware issue.

Placement dramatically changes performance. Moving the tracker to the bicep instead of the wrist reduces the effective storage interval to 2.4 seconds with near-zero bias against an ECG reference. Google does not disclose this placement sensitivity in consumer documentation. [4]

Heart rate variability (HRV)

HRV is reported using RMSSD (root mean square of successive differences between consecutive RR intervals). RMSSD is the standard short-term HRV metric and correlates with parasympathetic nervous system tone. The device derives HRV from the longest continuous sleep period in the past 24 hours; sessions shorter than 3 hours are excluded from calculation. [5]

04 SpO2 Sensor

Blood Oxygen Measurement

SpO2 measurement uses the red and infrared channels on the optical sensor. [1] The underlying physics: oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO₂) absorbs red light (~660 nm) weakly and IR strongly; deoxygenated hemoglobin (Hb) does the reverse. The ratio of pulsatile red-to-IR reflectance — calibrated against a lookup table derived from clinical measurements — gives the SpO2 estimate. This is standard transmissive or reflective pulse oximetry applied to a wrist optical geometry.

MODULE ARCHITECTURE UNKNOWN

Whether the red/IR pair for SpO2 is integrated into a single optical module alongside the green HR LEDs, or is a physically separate die/component, cannot be determined from public sources. No teardown or FCC internal photo exists to confirm module count, PCB footprint, or component identity.

05 Skin Temperature Sensor

Skin Temperature — What It Measures and What It Doesn't

The skin temperature sensor takes a reading every minute during wear. [6] This measures peripheral wrist surface temperature — the thermal equilibrium of the skin in contact with the tracker backplate. This is not core body temperature. Peripheral skin temperature is heavily influenced by:

  • Ambient air temperature (vasoconstriction in cold = lower skin temp)
  • Cardiovascular state (vasodilation from exercise or fever = higher skin temp)
  • Sweat evaporation (cooling effect)
  • Wrist pressure and contact area
  • Sleep stage (skin temp rises during NREM, drops at REM onset)

Google's documentation explicitly states that significant ambient temperature changes will degrade tracking accuracy. [6] The device requires a minimum of 3 days of wear to establish a personal baseline and uses up to 30 days of rolling data to continuously recalibrate that baseline. [6] Relative deviations from baseline are more interpretable than absolute values.

SENSOR TYPE UNKNOWN

Whether the temperature sensor is an NTC thermistor, a MEMS thermopile, a dedicated silicon temperature IC (e.g., TMP series), or integrated into the PPG ASIC is not disclosed by Google and cannot be determined without a teardown. No manufacturer or part number is available from any public source.

06 IMU — Accelerometer & Gyroscope

Inertial Measurement Unit

The Fitbit Air contains a 3-axis accelerometer and a gyroscope — a combined 6-axis IMU. [1] The accelerometer drives step counting, activity recognition (walk / run / swim detection), and sleep staging (wrist movement patterns during sleep transitions). The gyroscope adds rotational-rate data, enabling better discrimination between meaningful motion events and stationary arm movements that would otherwise generate false steps.

NO PART NUMBER AVAILABLE

Manufacturer (Bosch Sensortec, STMicroelectronics, TDK InvenSense, or other), part number, full-scale g-range (±2 g / ±4 g / ±8 g / ±16 g), output data rate, and noise density are all unknown. No teardown or FCC component-marking photograph is publicly available for this device.

Known firmware issues at launch included step counting inaccuracies and automatic workout mis-detection (running sessions labeled as generic activity). A firmware update was released approximately seven weeks post-launch to address step and sleep tracking bugs. [9] Whether the root cause was IMU register configuration, algorithm tuning, or both is not disclosed by Google.

Charging

The Fitbit Air charges via USB-C, compliant with USB Power Delivery. Supported input: 5 V DC (max 3 A) or 9 V DC (max 2 A). [2] Fast charging achieves approximately one day of runtime from a 5-minute charge; full charge takes roughly 90 minutes. [1]

CELL DIMENSIONS UNKNOWN

Physical cell dimensions (L × W × H in mm) are not disclosed in any public document. The ~0.9 g mass from the regulatory filing is the only physical attribute confirmed. A 34.5 mAh Li-Ion cell inside an 8.3 mm thick device is consistent with a thin pouch cell geometry, but dimensions require teardown confirmation.
