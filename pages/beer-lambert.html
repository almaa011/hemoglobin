The Beer–Lambert Law

How light gets absorbed by matter — the equation behind every photometer, pulse oximeter, and NIR sensor you will ever build.

No prior optics or chemistry assumed. If you know Ohm's law, you already understand the structure of this one.

↓ start reading
01 Core idea

Light going through stuff gets absorbed

Shine a beam of light at one face of a glass of red wine. Less light comes out the other side. Some of it was absorbed by the wine molecules along the way. Pour a second glass — stack them in the path of the beam — and even less comes out. Fill the glass with a more concentrated wine, same thing.

The Beer–Lambert law turns that intuition into a precise, quantitative relationship. It says:

The amount of light absorbed depends linearly on two things: how concentrated the absorbing substance is, and how much material the light has to travel through. Double the concentration → double the absorption. Double the path length → double the absorption.

That is the entire idea. Everything else — the equation, the logarithm, the units — is bookkeeping around this core relationship. When you understand why those details exist, the law becomes obvious rather than arbitrary.

Why does absorption happen at all?

Molecules absorb light when a photon's energy matches the gap between two of their electronic or vibrational energy levels. The photon disappears; its energy excites the molecule. Because each molecule has a characteristic set of energy gaps, it absorbs strongly at certain wavelengths and barely at others — which is why hemoglobin looks red (it absorbs green and blue light strongly) and why dissolved oxygen does not absorb visible light at all (its energy levels don't match visible photons).

For the purposes of measurement, the key practical fact is: the amount absorbed is proportional to how many absorbing molecules the light beam encounters — which depends on both concentration and the length of the path.

02 The equation

A = ε · c · l

A = ε · c · l
Beer–Lambert Law  ·  also written A = εcl or log(I₀/I) = εcl

Four variables. Each one is a real, measurable physical quantity:

A
Absorbance
dimensionless (AU)
How much light was absorbed. Defined as log₁₀(I₀ / I) — the log ratio of incoming to outgoing intensity. You derive this from your measurements; it is the left-hand side you are usually trying to predict or use.
ε
Molar absorptivity
L · mol⁻¹ · cm⁻¹
How strongly this particular substance absorbs at this particular wavelength. A property of the molecule — you look it up or measure it. Haemoglobin at 660 nm has a very different ε than at 940 nm; that difference is what lets pulse oximetry work.
c
Concentration
mol · L⁻¹ (M)
How many moles of the absorbing substance are dissolved per litre. In blood work you might encounter g/dL or g/L — you need to convert to molar units using the substance's molecular weight before the equation is consistent.
l
Path length
cm
The distance the light beam travels through the absorbing medium. In a bench cuvette this is a known, fixed number (typically 1 cm). In tissue optics — a PPG probe or near-infrared spectroscopy — this is unknown and variable, which is the central engineering challenge.

The logarithm note: Absorbance is defined as log₁₀(I₀/I), not I₀/I. This is deliberate — see section 05 for the explanation. For now, just note that A is what you compute from measured light intensities, and ε · c · l is what predicts it.

03 Physical meaning

What each variable actually represents

Equations become intuitive when you can map each symbol to something you can picture or feel. Here are four everyday analogies, one per variable.

☕
Concentration · c
Espresso vs. americano
An espresso is a concentrated shot — dark, opaque, absorbs nearly all the light. An americano is the same shot diluted with water — lighter, more light gets through. Same molecules, different count per cubic centimetre. That count is concentration.
🌫️
Path length · l
Fog on a motorway
Fog with a 50 m visibility is the same fog whether you're 10 m or 200 m into it — but more of it lies between you and the car ahead at 200 m. Path length is simply the depth of material the photons have to cross. Longer path = more encounters with absorbing molecules.
🔴
Molar absorptivity · ε
Coloured glass filters
A red glass filter passes red wavelengths and kills green. A neutral density filter absorbs all wavelengths equally. The material itself determines which wavelengths it catches — that selectivity is ε. Same concentration, same path, but different molecule → completely different absorbance. This is why wavelength choice matters so much in sensor design.
💡
Absorbance · A
The output you measure
A is not a material property — it is your measurement. Put a photodiode at both ends of the cuvette, record I₀ (lamp only) and I (lamp through sample), compute log₁₀(I₀/I). That number is A. Everything else in the equation predicts it from the sample's physical properties.

The Ohm's law parallel

If you squint, the structure is the same as V = I · R. You have an output quantity (A / V), a material property (ε / R), and two geometry/source variables (c and l / I and geometry of the circuit). In both laws, doubling any one of the right-hand-side variables doubles the left-hand-side output — they are both linear, proportional relationships in their respective domains.

05 The log scale

Why is there a logarithm in the definition?

Every layer of solution removes a fixed fraction of the remaining light — not a fixed absolute amount. If the first 1 mm of a solution absorbs 50% of incoming photons, the next 1 mm absorbs 50% of what is left (25% of the original), and the next 1 mm absorbs 50% of that (12.5%), and so on. The transmitted intensity falls exponentially with depth:

I = I₀ · 10−εcl
Transmitted intensity — an exponential decay in depth

That exponential is inconvenient: if you plot transmitted intensity against concentration, you get a curve that is hard to read and harder to calibrate against. But if you take the base-10 log of both sides, the exponential collapses and you get a straight line:

Linear vs. log domain comparison

A = log₁₀(I₀/I) = ε·c·l is a linear function of concentration. That matters enormously in practice: a straight-line calibration curve is easy to fit, easy to extend, and has a clear physical meaning. The log transform converts a law of fractions into a law of sums.

The acoustic parallel: The decibel (dB) scale for sound pressure does exactly the same thing — takes the log of a ratio so that doubling the amplitude corresponds to a fixed dB step, not an ever-changing fraction. Beer–Lambert absorbance is the optical equivalent of the decibel.

Practical consequence for sensor design

When you design a spectrophotometric measurement system, you want to operate in the linear (absorbance) domain. That means your signal-processing chain computes A = log₁₀(I₀/I) as early as possible. Fitting models, regression, and machine learning features should all be built from A values — not from raw intensities, which sit on the curved exponential side of the transform.

06 Limitations

When the law breaks down

The Beer–Lambert law is a limiting approximation — it holds well under specific conditions, and fails predictably outside them. Knowing the failure modes is as important as knowing the law itself.

📈
High concentration
Above roughly 0.01 mol/L, neighbouring molecules begin to interact electrostatically, altering each other's absorption characteristics. ε is no longer constant — it decreases with increasing concentration. The absorbance-vs-concentration curve bends downward. In practice: dilute your samples, or build a non-linear calibration over the actual operating range.
💫
Light scattering
The law assumes light is only lost by absorption. In turbid media — tissue, blood, milk, emulsions — photons are also scattered, changing direction without being absorbed. Scattered photons that exit the sample sideways are counted as "absorbed" by a naïve measurement. Tissue optics requires the modified Beer–Lambert law, which introduces a differential pathlength factor (DPF) to account for the extended path that scattered photons travel.
🔦
Stray light
If your detector receives any light that did not pass through the sample — whether from imperfect optical isolation, ambient leakage, or instrument stray light — it inflates I artificially, making A read lower than true. This effect is most damaging at high absorbances, where I is very small and even a tiny stray contribution is proportionally large.
🌈
Polychromatic light
The law strictly applies to monochromatic light — a single wavelength. A broadband source illuminating a sample whose ε varies across the bandwidth produces a mixed signal. If the sample's absorption is not flat across the bandwidth, the apparent absorbance is a nonlinear blend of multiple Beer–Lambert relationships. Narrow-band LEDs or tight bandpass filters mitigate this.
⚗️
Chemical equilibria
If the absorbing species is in chemical equilibrium with another form — for example, haemoglobin cycling between oxy and deoxy states, or a pH indicator dissociating — the effective concentration of each species depends on conditions beyond the nominal total concentration. You must know the equilibrium distribution to apply the law correctly.
📏
Unknown path length
In reflectance geometries — including every wearable PPG sensor — the path length l is neither fixed nor known. Light scatters through tissue along curved, variable paths that depend on optical properties, probe geometry, skin pigmentation, and vessel depth. This is not a failure of the Beer–Lambert law; it is a geometric complication that requires separate characterisation or a ratiometric approach that cancels l.

Bottom line: In well-controlled bench chemistry (dilute solution, 1 cm cuvette, monochromatic light, no scattering), Beer–Lambert is highly accurate. In complex real-world media — tissue, whole blood, suspensions — it provides the conceptual framework but must be extended or calibrated empirically to account for scattering and geometry.
