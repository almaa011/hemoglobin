Beer–Lambert Law
for Pulse Oximetry

How a 19th-century chemistry law became the foundation of every SpO₂ sensor — and why tissue breaks every assumption it makes. This page covers the standard and modified Beer–Lambert equations, the molar extinction coefficients that drive LED wavelength choice, the ratio-of-ratios SpO₂ derivation, how the AFE4490 signal chain is structured around these equations, and the four failure modes that expose the limits of the 2-wavelength approach.

2026-06-13 · Broad overview · Hardware-focused · Moderate biology depth

01 / The Law

Beer–Lambert: light attenuation through matter

Before tissue, scattering, and hemoglobin — start with the cleanest case: a single absorber in solution, a monochromatic LED, straight-line propagation.

When a photon beam passes through a medium, molecules absorb photons proportionally to their concentration and the path length traversed. Beer–Lambert formalizes this:[1]

Beer–Lambert Law
A = ε · c · L
A = absorbance = log₁₀(I₀ / I)  [dimensionless] ε = molar extinction coefficient  [cm⁻¹/(mol·L⁻¹)] c = absorber concentration  [mol·L⁻¹] L = optical path length  [cm]

In hardware terms: I₀ is the radiant flux emitted by the LED before it enters the sample; I is the flux that exits and lands on the photodiode. Absorbance A = log₁₀(I₀/I) links your photodiode current reading back to the hemoglobin concentration you actually want to measure.[1]

The equation bakes in four assumptions, all of which are violated when you shine light through a fingertip:

AssumptionMeaningStatus in tissue
Monochromatic lightSingle wavelength onlyLEDs have finite spectral width
Dilute solutionAbsorbers don't interactBlood is not dilute
Homogeneous mediumSame composition throughoutMultiple tissue layers, multiple absorbers
No scatteringAll attenuation is absorptionScattering is 30–93× stronger than absorption[9]

Despite all of this, Beer–Lambert is still the mathematical skeleton of every pulse oximeter. The rest of this page explains how engineers work around each broken assumption.

02 / Tissue

Why tissue breaks the law — and the fix

Scattering dominates absorption

In the near-infrared optical window (650–950 nm), scattering is not a minor perturbation — it is the dominant photon–tissue interaction. At 660 nm in skin, the reduced scattering coefficient μs′ (think of it as a diffusion-broadening coefficient per unit length) is 1.8–2.8 mm⁻¹, versus an absorption coefficient μa (resistive loss per unit length) of only 0.03–0.06 mm⁻¹. That gives a scattering-to-absorption ratio of 30 to 93:1.[9]

Standard Beer–Lambert assumes photons travel in a straight line from source to detector. In tissue they do not. They bounce off collagen fibers, cell membranes, and organelles, executing a random walk. The effective optical path is far longer than the physical source-to-detector distance — by a factor called the DPF.

Photon path through skin (reflectance geometry) LED and PD on same surface, ~3–5 mm separation

Photons take a curved random-walk ("banana") path through tissue. r is the straight-line source-detector distance; the actual mean photon path is r × DPF. Dashed grey path = photons scattered so far off-axis they never reach the PD (the G loss term in modified Beer–Lambert). Scattering is 30–93× stronger than absorption at 660 nm.[9]

Multiple absorbers

Tissue is not a single-species solution. Each layer has its own absorbers: oxyhemoglobin (HbO₂) and deoxyhemoglobin (Hb) in blood, melanin in the epidermis, water throughout, and lipids in adipose tissue. Standard Beer–Lambert handles one absorber. Tissue has at least five.

The Modified Beer–Lambert Law

Tissue scattering adds two correction terms to the standard equation:[3]

Modified Beer–Lambert Law
A = ε · c · r · DPF + G
r = physical source-to-detector distance [cm] DPF = Differential Pathlength Factor [dimensionless] — the "detour factor" G = scattering loss: photons that never reach the detector [OD = optical density = absorbance units]

DPF is the ratio of mean photon path length to physical separation. A DPF of 6 means photons travel 6× the geometric distance before reaching the detector. In functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS — an adjacent field that uses the same modified Beer–Lambert framework for brain monitoring), it has been common practice to use a DPF value between 3 and 6, with DPF = 6 commonly adopted as a practical approximation for adult continuous-wave fNIRS data.[5]

Monte Carlo simulations of layered human skin give DPF at 650 nm ranging from 7.73 (0.5 mm separation) to 10.24 (8 mm). At 950 nm, DPF ranges from 9.32 (0.5 mm) down to 4.45 (8 mm).[4] DPF is a function of both wavelength and sensor geometry — not a universal constant.

Hardware implication

DPF is wavelength-dependent because scattering in the NIR scales as ~λ⁻⁰·⁷.[10] This means the effective optical path is longer at 660 nm than at 940 nm. The modified Beer–Lambert equation includes a path-length ratio (l₂/l₁) to correct for this asymmetry when computing SpO₂.[6] In practice, manufacturers absorb this into their empirical calibration curve rather than computing it analytically.

The G term accounts for photons scattered so far off-axis they never reach the detector — pure insertion loss. When taking differential measurements (ΔA between two states), G cancels. In absolute SpO₂ measurement it does not cancel exactly, which is another reason empirical calibration is always required.[3]

03 / Spectra

Absorption spectra — why 660 nm and 940 nm

The two LED wavelengths are not arbitrary. They exploit a specific crossover in the molar extinction coefficients of HbO₂ and Hb.

HbO₂ vs. Hb — molar extinction coefficients cm⁻¹/(mol·L⁻¹) · Source: Prahl/OMLC[2]

Filled circles are verified data points from the OMLC hemoglobin table.[2] Curve shapes between marked points are stylized for readability — intermediate values are not cited. Hb (deoxy) at 660 nm: 3226 cm⁻¹/M. HbO₂ (oxy) at 940 nm: 1214 cm⁻¹/M. Isobestic ~805 nm: both species absorb equally.

Verified extinction coefficients at the two working wavelengths, from the Oregon Medical Laser Center database (Prahl, compiled from Gratzer and Kollias):[2]

Species660 nm (red)940 nm (NIR)Ratio (660/940)
HbO₂ (oxy) 319.6 cm⁻¹/M 1214 cm⁻¹/M 0.26
Hb (deoxy) 3226.56 cm⁻¹/M 693.44 cm⁻¹/M 4.65

At 660 nm: Hb absorbs roughly 10× more than HbO₂. At 940 nm: the roles partially reverse — HbO₂ absorbs ~1.75× more than Hb. This large opposing contrast at each wavelength is what makes the two-equation system mathematically well-conditioned: small SpO₂ changes produce large, measurable changes in the relative signals.

The isobestic point

Around 805 nm, HbO₂ and Hb have equal extinction coefficients — the isobestic point.[11] The OMLC table shows the crossover falling between 800 and 806 nm; the commonly cited value of 805 nm comes from secondary clinical sources.[2][11] At this wavelength, absorbance is proportional to total hemoglobin regardless of oxygenation fraction — useful as a calibration reference point independent of SpO₂.

Why two wavelengths are required

Two unknowns — [HbO₂] and [Hb] — require two equations. Each wavelength provides one Beer–Lambert equation, yielding a 2×2 linear system solvable for both concentrations simultaneously.[6] One wavelength alone cannot separate the two species.

04 / SpO₂

The ratio-of-ratios — Beer–Lambert becomes SpO₂

The photodiode sees two superimposed signals: a large DC baseline from static tissue, and a tiny AC pulsatile component from the arterial heartbeat. Beer–Lambert applies only to the AC component.

AC vs. DC decomposition of the PPG signal Scale exaggerated for visibility — AC is ~0.5–1% of total signal

The pulsatile (AC) component is typically only 0.5–1% of the total photocurrent.[12] The remaining 99%+ is the DC pedestal: static tissue, venous blood, non-pulsatile arterial baseline. AC/DC × 100% = Perfusion Index (PI). Values below 0.5% indicate low perfusion where accuracy degrades.[16]

The AC/DC ratio normalizes out LED drive current, fiber coupling, and fixed tissue attenuation. The SpO₂ calculation uses the ratio of these ratios across the two wavelengths:[6]

Ratio-of-Ratios
R = (AC₆₆₀ / DC₆₆₀) / (AC₉₄₀ / DC₉₄₀)

Solving the two modified Beer–Lambert equations simultaneously for the oxygenation fraction yields a theoretical SpO₂:[6]

Theoretical SpO₂ from Beer–Lambert
SpO₂ = (εHb,660 − R·εHb,940) / [R(εHbO₂,940 − εHb,940) + (εHb,660 − εHbO₂,660)]
A path-length ratio correction (l₂/l₁) is required when DPF differs between wavelengths[6]

Empirical calibration — why theory isn't enough

The theoretical derivation assumes known DPF and perfectly monochromatic LEDs. In reality, DPF is subject-variable, LEDs have finite spectral width (requiring a spectrally weighted effective ε), and tissue has absorbers beyond HbO₂ and Hb. So manufacturers use an empirical R–SpO₂ lookup table instead.

Calibration is performed on healthy volunteers whose SaO₂ (arterial oxygen saturation measured invasively from a blood gas analyser — the gold-standard reference; SpO₂ is the non-invasive pulse oximeter estimate of this value) is stepped from 100% down to approximately 70% using a hypoxic breathing protocol (subjects breathe a reduced-oxygen gas mixture under medical supervision). R is recorded at each controlled saturation level.[13]

A widely-cited linear approximation is:[14]

Empirical linear approximation (Webster's formula)
SpO₂ ≈ 110 − 25·R
R = 0.4 → SpO₂ ≈ 100%  |  R = 1.0 → SpO₂ ≈ 85%  |  R = 1.6 → SpO₂ ≈ 70% A second cited approximation: SpO₂ = −39.4·R + 112.9[13] Both are rough linear fits; device firmware uses proprietary nonlinear lookup tables
Calibration validity range

Empirical calibration data only covers 70–100% SpO₂. Below 70%, values are extrapolated — not measured. Pulse oximeter readings are typically within ±2% accuracy compared to arterial blood gas analysis, within ±4% in 95% of cases.[15]

05 / Hardware

Hardware implications — from Beer–Lambert to signal chain

SpO₂ AFE signal chain Beer–Lambert variables mapped to hardware blocks

I₀ is set by LED drive current. Tissue is the absorbing medium (ε, c, r·DPF, G). I is the photodiode current. TIA converts I → V; ADC digitizes it. AC/DC separation and R calculation happen in firmware.

The dynamic range problem

The AC pulsatile signal is only 0.5–1% of total photocurrent — 100–200× smaller than the DC pedestal.[12] If the TIA is sized for the full DC without headroom compression, the ADC spends essentially all of its full-scale range on the DC level and the pulsatile signal is buried near the quantization noise floor.

Perfusion Index (PI) quantifies this: PI = AC/DC × 100%. At PI = 0.5%, the signal of interest is 1/200th of full scale. Below PI = 0.5%, SpO₂ accuracy degrades significantly.[16]

AFE4490 response to the dynamic range problem

Texas Instruments AFE4490: 22-bit ADC, programmable TIA gain (7 feedback resistor settings — values 10 kΩ to 1 MΩ confirmed in the AFE4400 family datasheet, same architecture[8]), analog ambient light cancellation DAC (1–10 µA adjustable), 110 dB dynamic range, LED drive programmable at 50/75/100/150/200 mA ranges. Noise-free resolution at 5 µA PD current: 13.5 bits, input-referred noise: 50 pARMS.[7]

Ambient light cancellation

The AFE4490 includes an analog ambient-light cancellation DAC (1–10 µA adjustable) that injects a programmable current opposing the photodiode's ambient-light DC component before the TIA stage, reducing the DC burden before digitization.[7] A current-driven 22-bit DAC provides the cancellation current.[17] LED duty cycles as low as 2–10% are used in low-power designs.[17]

06 / Failure Modes

Where Beer–Lambert breaks down in practice

A 2-wavelength pulse oximeter solves a 2×2 system: two equations, two unknowns. Anything that introduces a third unknown — or corrupts the AC/DC measurement — breaks the system.

Motion artifacts

Motion causes venous blood in tissue to shift, creating optical signals that mimic arterial pulsations but are not synchronous with the cardiac cycle.[15] These corrupt the AC amplitude and DC baseline estimates at both wavelengths, randomizing R. Comparative testing across three commercial pulse oximeters showed motion increased SpO₂ ARMS error by 30–78%: from a baseline of 1.79–2.40 up to 2.45–4.15 under motion conditions.[18]

Low perfusion

When PI falls below 0.5%, signal quality is considered poor.[16] At PI < 1.0, SpO₂ error across three commercial devices ranged from 3.24 to 4.76 — already above the ±2% accuracy target. At PI 0.1–0.3, errors reached 4.52–5.55. At ultra-low PI, some devices began reporting signal loss entirely.[18]

Carboxyhemoglobin (CO-Hb)

CO-Hb has nearly identical absorption to HbO₂ at 660 nm, so the 2-wavelength system counts it as oxyhemoglobin. But CO-Hb absorbs very little at 940 nm — so it contributes to the numerator of R without contributing to the denominator. Result: CO-Hb is invisible to a standard pulse oximeter, and a patient with significant CO poisoning may show SpO₂ = 98% while their true functional saturation is far lower. The error grows proportionally with CO-Hb level.[19]

Methemoglobin (Met-Hb)

Met-Hb absorbs strongly at both 660 nm and 940 nm. As Met-Hb concentration rises, it pulls the absorbance ratio toward R ≈ 1.0. On the empirical calibration curve, R = 1.0 maps to SpO₂ ≈ 85% (consistent with SpO₂ = 110 − 25×1.0 = 85). So Met-Hb causes SpO₂ readings to plateau near 85% regardless of actual saturation — whether the patient is truly at 98% or 30%. At Met-Hb ≈ 30–35%, this plateau is well established.[20]

The two-equation limit

CO-Hb and Met-Hb are third and fourth hemoglobin species the 2×2 system has no equations for. Detecting them requires a co-oximeter — a multi-wavelength device that adds equations to resolve each species individually.[21]

Skin tone and melanin

Melanin is a broadband absorber concentrated in the epidermis. Monte Carlo simulations of three skin types at 660 nm and 3 mm source-detector separation show: light skin detects 1,791 photons per million launched; dark skin detects only 55 — a 32.5× reduction. At 940 nm, the impact is minimal (<0.2% difference).[22]

In a perfect system, melanin wouldn't bias R because it attenuates AC and DC equally at each wavelength, and the ratio cancels it. In practice, the 32× photon reduction at 660 nm raises shot noise on the AC component, increasing variance in the SpO₂ estimate. Clinical data indicates dark-skinned patients were three times as likely as white patients to have low oxygen saturation missed by standard pulse oximeters.[15]
