01  Wire type & construction

What goes inside the cable

For 1.5 m EEG leads, the standard answer is shielded twisted pair (STP) — but "shielded" is doing a lot of work in that phrase and needs unpacking. Start with the conductor, then the geometry, then the shield.

Conductor material

EEG frequencies (0.1–100 Hz) are so low that skin effect and surface resistivity are completely irrelevant — a copper wire at 100 Hz has negligible AC resistance. The reason commercial EEG leadwires use silver-plated copper (AWG 30–32 stranded) has nothing to do with frequency response.[4] The real reasons are:

  • Corrosion resistance. Silver-plated conductors resist chloride attack from sweat and conductive gel. Bare silver corrodes via Ag + Cl⁻ → AgCl, causing partial electrical disconnect in humid environments within hours.[5] Gold plating extends this by ~70×.[5]
  • Low half-cell potential. Electrode–electrolyte potential stability matters at the electrode end, not the wire end. The wire conductor is not in contact with the patient — silver plating on the wire is a shield against oxidation of the conductor strands inside the jacket, not a biopotential concern.
  • Plain copper, nickel, and steel are avoided for galvanic stability and corrosion reasons, not for signal fidelity at EEG frequencies.[4]

Practical takeaway: at 0.1–100 Hz, a plain copper conductor is electrically identical to silver for signal transmission. If cost-constrained, spend the money on better shielding, not on silver plating the wire.

Twisted pair vs coaxial vs flat

The differential signal in an EEG lead is the voltage between two electrode sites — say, Fp1 and its reference. The pair of wires carrying those signals must be routed together so that external interference couples equally to both and is subtracted by the instrumentation amplifier. Twisting enforces this:

Geometry comparison for EEG differential pairs

STP is the standard for passive EEG electrodes routed as differential pairs. Coaxial works when a unity-gain buffer (active electrode) is built directly into the electrode housing, converting the high-impedance node to low-impedance before the run.

Twisting cancels inductive pickup because every half-twist swaps which wire is closer to the interference source — the induced voltages in alternating loops cancel.[2] The twist pitch (turns per metre) matters: tighter twists cancel higher spatial frequencies of interference. For 1.5 m EEG leads, a minimum of 2–4 twists per centimetre is typical.

02  Shielding

Passive shield, driven shield — and why it matters which you use

This section contains the most counterintuitive result in EEG cable design: a passively-grounded shield can make 50/60 Hz interference worse, not better, when your source impedance is high.[9] Understanding why leads directly to the right solution.

What a passive shield does — and where it fails

A foil or braid shield around the twisted pair intercepts electric-field interference before it reaches the signal conductors. For power-line frequencies (50/60 Hz), it is an electrostatic screen. Grounding it at one end (typically at the amplifier/PCB end) creates the return path for induced charge without forming a ground loop.[24]

Here is where the problem enters: the shield and the signal conductor inside it form a capacitor. For 1.5 m of cable at ~7 pF/ft, that is approximately 350 pF of capacitance from signal wire to shield. At EEG electrode impedances of 1–10 kΩ, this capacitor forms a potential divider with the electrode impedance.

Why a passively grounded shield backfires at high electrode impedance
Thevenin equivalent · 50 Hz
The 350 pF problem. An external noise source couples 10 nA of leakage current into the signal line. With a passive grounded shield, the cable capacitance (350 pF for 1.5 m) to the shield shunts that current to ground — but the impedance of 350 pF at 50 Hz is 9 MΩ. At 5 kΩ electrode impedance, the current instead drops most of its voltage across the electrode, not the shield. BioSemi measured 200 µV p-p interference in this scenario.[9] That is 200× the signal.
Conflict surfaced — Passive shield grounding

IEC 60601-1-2 guidance recommends grounding the shield at one end for 50/60 Hz applications.[24] The BioSemi analysis[9] shows this is insufficient for high-impedance electrode sources: the cable capacitance creates a voltage-divider problem that can produce 200 µV p-p artifact. Both statements are correct in their context. The IEC guidance is a general EMC rule; the BioSemi result is specific to high-Z biopotential measurement. For EEG, driven shielding solves the conflict.

The driven (bootstrap) shield

A driven shield — also called a guard drive or bootstrap shield — holds the shield conductor at exactly the same voltage as the signal wire inside it, tracked in real time. Because the voltage difference between signal and shield is (ideally) zero, the capacitance between them is effectively neutralized: no current flows across a capacitor at zero voltage differential, so the cable capacitance disappears from the circuit.[7]

Driven shield — concept and implementation
bootstrap guard · ADS1299 BIAS_SHD
The guard follower circuit. A unity-gain buffer (or the BIAS_SHD output of the ADS1299EEG-FE[6]) drives the shield to the common-mode voltage of the differential pair. External noise that would have coupled through the cable capacitance instead sees the same voltage on both sides of the capacitor and cannot cause current to flow. Measured result using an INA116-based implementation: SNR improved from 1.05 (commercial system) to 16.09; 50 Hz interference eliminated without any post-acquisition filtering.[8]

Conductive polymer (carbon-loaded polyethylene) jacket material reduces triboelectric noise specifically because it has similar charge affinity to adjacent materials — the triboelectric series separation is minimized, reducing charge generation under cable flexion.[15]

For the driven shield to work, only one end of the shield should make contact with the drive signal (at the PCB connector). The other end (at the electrode) is left floating or connected through a high-impedance node. Never tie both ends of the shield to the drive output — this creates an unintended current return path.

03  Electrical characteristics

Capacitance, resistance, and the CMRR math

Cable capacitance and electrode impedance interaction

For 1.5 m of low-capacitance medical-grade twisted pair (target: 6.1–8.7 pF/ft[3]), the total conductor-to-conductor capacitance is approximately 7 pF/ft × 5 ft ≈ 35 pF. The total conductor-to-shield capacitance will be roughly 2–3× that for a well-built STP. These numbers matter because they interact with electrode impedance to set the −3 dB frequency of an RC low-pass filter formed by electrode–skin resistance and cable capacitance.

The electrode–cable RC filter

f-3dB = 1 / (2π × Zelec × Ccable)

At Zelec = 5 kΩ, Ccable = 100 pF (conductor-to-shield, 1.5 m): f-3dB = 318 kHz — well above the 100 Hz EEG band. No rolloff problem.

The risk is not signal rolloff but CMRR degradation from capacitance imbalance between the two legs of the differential pair. If one wire picks up 50 pF to shield and the other picks up 60 pF, the common-mode current divides unequally — 10 pF of imbalance appears as a differential signal at the input.

CMRR degradation — the dominant mechanism

The instrumentation amplifier (IA) at the heart of the ADS1299 input stage rejects common-mode signals by subtraction. For subtraction to work perfectly, both inputs must see identical source impedances. In practice, two sources of asymmetry destroy CMRR:

1. Electrode impedance mismatch (ΔZE). The CMRR degradation caused by mismatched electrode impedances follows:[11]

CMRR degradation vs electrode impedance mismatch
50 Hz · ADS1299 intrinsic CMRR = 90 dB
CMRReffective ≈ CMRRamp − 20 log(ΔZE / Zin).[11] A 5 Ω impedance imbalance at the source degrades a nominally 80 dB amplifier CMRR to 40 dB.[12] If one electrode sits at 5 kΩ and a neighbor at 50 kΩ — a 10:1 mismatch — the 60 Hz pickup at both sites is amplified differently and appears as a differential artifact that no shielding can cure.[13]

2. Cable capacitance imbalance between the two legs. Any asymmetry in the capacitive coupling from each signal wire to the shield (or to adjacent wires) presents as a different source impedance on each leg. The RLD (right-leg drive) wire routed inside the same cable bundle also contributes: parasitic capacitance from the RLD wire to channel wires allows the common-mode drive signal to bypass the patient and couple unequally into channels.[10]

The hard truth about CMRR

Good amplifier specs do not guarantee clean recordings. The whole signal path — cable, electrode impedance, shield, RLD topology — must be analyzed together. A well-specified amplifier with poorly matched electrode impedances or an asymmetric cable bundle will produce worse 50/60 Hz rejection than a mediocre amplifier with well-prepared electrodes.[14] This is the most important practical takeaway from the BioSemi analysis.

Wire resistance

AWG 32 copper wire resistance is approximately 164 Ω/km, or 0.246 Ω for a 1.5 m run. At EEG frequencies and electrode impedances of 1–10 kΩ, this is negligible — the wire resistance is <0.025% of the electrode impedance and introduces no measurable signal attenuation or additional noise. Wire DC resistance is simply not a design constraint for 1.5 m EEG leads.

04  EMI & noise

Three noise sources — and how the wire amplifies or rejects each

1. Mains interference (50/60 Hz)

A 1.5 m electrode lead in a typical lab environment acts as an antenna with effective area determined by the loop formed between the two differential wires. Tighter twisting reduces this loop area, reducing magnetic coupling. The driven shield addresses electric-field coupling (capacitive). The two mechanisms are independent:

  • Magnetic pickup (inductive) → minimized by tight twist pitch and symmetric routing. The loop area of a twisted pair decreases with turns/metre.
  • Electric-field pickup (capacitive) → minimized by the driven shield, which makes the shield appear to have zero voltage difference from the wire — external electric fields can no longer drive current through the cable capacitance into the signal path.

Routing both wires of the differential pair together and minimizing separation between them is the most effective passive technique. Any asymmetry in the routing (one wire loops closer to a power supply) breaks the common-mode assumption and converts mains pickup to differential artifact.

2. Triboelectric noise

When the cable flexes, the inner insulation slides against the braid or shield, generating a charge at the insulator-conductor interface by contact electrification (the triboelectric effect). This charge appears as a voltage step or spike superimposed on the signal — it is mechanically-induced, not electrostatically-induced, and shielding does nothing to prevent it.[19]

The AAMI ANSI EC53 standard requires triboelectric noise below 50 µV peak-to-valley for ECG/EEG trunk cables and patient leadwires.[16] Cables meeting this spec use:

  • Carbon-loaded polyethylene (CLPE) inner jacket. A semiconductive layer between the conductor and the outer shield creates a path that slowly dissipates charge before it accumulates. The carbon doping keeps the layer at ~106–108 Ω/sq — resistive enough not to short the signal but conductive enough to drain triboelectric charge.
  • Matching triboelectric series materials. Materials with similar charge affinity (≤10 nC/J apart in the triboelectric series) generate less charge on contact.[15]
  • Lubricated inter-layer contact. Some medical cable constructions include an interstitial lubricant or textile binder to reduce friction between jacket and shield.
Triboelectric noise amplitude depends on cable material properties, contact area, type of contact, and speed of relative motion — not purely on shielding.[19] A well-shielded cable with a standard PVC jacket can have worse triboelectric noise than an unshielded cable with CLPE.

3. Motion artifact

Cable motion artifact has two independent contributions: (a) triboelectric charge at the cable jacket, and (b) mechanical movement of the electrode–skin interface, which modulates the double-layer capacitance and half-cell potential at the electrode.

Finding conflict — Active electrodes and motion artifact

Conventional wisdom holds that active electrodes (unity-gain followers built into the electrode housing) reduce motion artifact by converting the high-impedance node to low-impedance before the cable run. A 2024 study (MDPI Sensors)[18] found active and passive electrodes produce comparable motion artifact. The most discriminating factor was cable length: short cables produced 7.64 µV artifact vs 46.03 µV with longer cables during jogging, regardless of electrode type. This challenges the conventional justification for active electrodes in motion-sensitive applications.

Cable sway during head movement produces a measurable 10% decrease in signal-to-noise ratio even with active electrode systems that include pre-amplification at the electrode.[17] For static (seated, clinical) EEG recordings this is a minor concern; for ambulatory or mobile EEG it is a design-driving constraint.

05  Connector & termination

From cable end to ADS1299 input pin

Connector standard: DIN 42802 / touch-proof safety connector

The de facto connector standard for EEG electrode leads is the DIN 42802 touch-proof safety connector (also referenced as IEC 60601-1 compliant). The specification requires:[23]

  • 1.5 mm diameter recessed metal pin — too small for a mains connector to mate with
  • Plastic housing such that a standard 30 N force probe cannot make electrical contact with the pin
  • All metal components recessed within the plastic housing

This connector is sometimes called a "safety connector," "touch-proof connector," or simply "1.5 mm EEG connector." It is used universally in clinical EEG and most research-grade systems.

ADS1299 input termination

The ADS1299EEG-FE evaluation board explicitly states it is not a reference design for EEG applications — it is for evaluation only.[22] Compliance with IEC 60601-2-26 is the manufacturer's responsibility. That said, the termination approach demonstrated on the evaluation board provides a verified starting point:

PCB layout considerations

  • Keep analog input traces short. After the ESD protection components, route to the ADS1299 INP/INN pins with minimum trace length. Each millimetre of PCB trace adds pF of parasitic capacitance to the input node.
  • Symmetric differential pair routing. After the connector, route both wires of each differential pair with identical trace length, layer, and via count. Asymmetric routing causes the same CMRR degradation as asymmetric cable.
  • Keep digital lines away from analog inputs. SPI clock and data lines switching at 4–8 MHz can couple into the µV-level analog inputs through substrate or magnetic coupling. A guard ring around the analog input area connected to analog ground helps.
  • Known failure mode: single-sample transient artifacts. ADS1299-based systems have exhibited single-sample, large-amplitude transients (~1 per 10,000 samples) linked to SPI communication and power domain interactions.[25] These are not cable-related but are worth monitoring in your validation testing.
