The whole thing in one breath

What this device actually does

It shines several colours of light into your finger, watches the light flicker in time with your pulse, and reads how much each colour dims. Because each colour is absorbed differently by hemoglobin, those readings — combined — point to one number: how much hemoglobin is in your blood, in grams per decilitre.

A regular pulse oximeter (the SpO₂ clip) does almost the same thing with just two colours. That works for oxygen saturation because saturation is a ratio — and ratios let most of the messy, unknown stuff cancel out. But total hemoglobin is an absolute amount, not a ratio. Nothing cancels. So you need more colours, more maths, and you still hit a wall the algorithm can't climb over.

The honest punchline

The physics sets a floor. Lab machines hit ±0.5 g/dL. A good optical device specs ±1 g/dL — but in the real world, independent studies land around ±2–3 g/dL. That gap isn't bad engineering. It's missing information: a light sensor pressed to skin never measures how far the photons actually travelled, and that unknown leaks straight into the answer. Build for screening and trends, not transfusion calls.

01  What the sensor sees

It's all in the flicker

PPG — photoplethysmography, "light + blood-volume + measuring" — is just this: an LED shines into tissue, a photodiode catches whatever light makes it back out, and you watch that light level wobble over time.

What makes it wobble is blood. Every heartbeat pushes a fresh slug of arterial blood into the finger. Blood absorbs light hard, so for a split second more light gets swallowed and the detected level dips. Between beats it recovers. That repeating dip-and-recover is the signal.

The PPG waveform on a scope
Two parts stacked together. The big steady band is the DC — all the stuff that doesn't pulse: skin, bone, fat, melanin, still venous blood. The tiny ripple riding on it is the AC — the pulsing arterial blood, and the only part carrying blood-composition info. Flip to "real scale" and watch the ripple almost vanish: in a fingertip the AC is about 1% of the DC. That 1% is the entire challenge.
Decoder · AC, DC & Perfusion Index

DC = the steady baseline. Tissue and static blood. Carries info about what's in the way.

AC = the pulsing part. Pure arterial blood. Carries info about what's in the blood.

Perfusion Index (PI) = AC ÷ DC × 100%. In a finger, ~1%. In cold or poorly-perfused hands it can drop below 0.5%, and your signal drowns.

The clever bit, first spotted by Takuo Aoyagi in the 1970s: only the arteries pulse. Everything else — skin, melanin, bone, the still venous blood — sits in both the "heartbeat in" and "heartbeat out" snapshots and subtracts away when you look at the change. So the difference between systole and diastole is almost pure arterial blood. That's why the little ripple, not the big baseline, is where hemoglobin hides.

The self-cancelling trick: the ratio R

Take the ripple size divided by the baseline at one colour — AC/DC. That single division quietly cancels a pile of unknowns you'd otherwise have to measure: how bright the LED is, how sensitive the photodiode is, how the sensor sits on the skin, the overall gain of your front-end. All of it scales both AC and DC equally, so it divides out.

Now divide one colour's AC/DC by another's, and you get the ratio-of-ratios, R. This is the workhorse of pulse oximetry. It turns a hopeless absolute-brightness problem into a ratio that mostly ignores your specific finger and just reports the shape of the blood's absorption. Hold onto that idea — it's exactly what makes the two-colour SpO₂ trick work, and exactly what isn't enough for hemoglobin.

02  Why the textbook law breaks

Light doesn't travel in straight lines through you

The clean version — the Beer–Lambert law — says absorbance equals how strongly a substance absorbs, times how much of it there is, times how far the light travelled. In a glass cuvette of clear liquid, perfect.

Tissue is not a cuvette. It's cloudy. Photons don't go straight from LED to photodiode — they ricochet off cells thousands of times and wander along long, curved, banana-shaped paths. Two problems fall out of that:

Where the photons actually go
reflectance geometry
The straight-line distance is a lie. The true path a photon travels is much longer than the gap between LED and detector — and you never know exactly how much longer for a given person. That unknown multiplier is the DPF, and it's the villain of this whole story.
Decoder · DPF & the modified law

DPF (Differential Pathlength Factor) = how many times longer the real photon path is than the straight-line LED-to-detector gap. Always > 1. Depends on wavelength, the person, and the body site.

The modified Beer–Lambert law just bolts two fixes onto the clean version: multiply the distance by DPF, and add a scatter-loss term. Tidy on paper. The catch: you don't know the DPF for the finger in front of you. It was never measured. That single missing number is the root of the irreducible error later on.

So how does PPG cope with an unknown pathlength? It cheats with time instead of space. By comparing the same finger at systole versus diastole (the AC trick again), every static term — including most of the geometry — is identical in both snapshots and cancels. What's left is governed only by the slug of arterial blood that just arrived. That's the whole reason the messy modified law collapses back into something usable: ΔA ≈ AC/DC.

03  Picking your colours

Six wavelengths, six jobs

Scroll back to the hero chart for a second — those two crossing curves are the map. Where you place an LED on that map decides what it tells you. Pick badly and your colours all say the same thing; pick well and each one pries apart a different unknown.

The hero of the set is 805 nm, the isosbestic crossing. There, oxygenated and deoxygenated hemoglobin absorb identically, so the reading no longer depends on oxygen — it's a clean shot at total hemoglobin. Every other colour is positioned relative to it: some below to catch deoxy-Hb, some above to catch oxy-Hb, one in the visible to gauge skin pigment.

Decoder · why two colours can't do this job

SpO₂ is a ratio — oxy ÷ (oxy + deoxy). Two colours, two species, and the unknown pathlength divides away. Done.

Total hemoglobin is an absolute amount. There's no second hemoglobin quantity to divide the unknowns into. Pathlength, scattering, melanin and water all stay in the equation — so you need extra colours, each adding one more independent clue, to pin them down. More unknowns ⇒ more wavelengths. That's the entire reason this device is harder than a pulse oximeter.

A practical edge: don't go past ~970 nm. Beyond there, water starts absorbing hard and swamps the hemoglobin signal. That's why 940 nm is the usual top of the band — deep penetration, but still safely under the water wall.

04  Raw photons → a number

The signal chain, end to end

One photodiode can't tell colours apart. So the front-end fires the LEDs one at a time, very fast, and samples the photodiode in lockstep with each flash. Only one LED is ever lit, so every sample belongs to exactly one colour. This is time-multiplexing, and it's what an integrated analog front-end (AFE) like the TI AFE4490/4404 is built to do.

From LED flash to hemoglobin estimate
six stages
Each stage strips away a problem. The AFE turns flickering photocurrent into clean per-colour numbers; software pulls out the ripple, throws away the junk beats, builds physics-based features, and a trained model maps them to grams per decilitre.

The four software steps, in plain terms

1 · Acquire. Fire LEDs in sequence; sample in sync. Between flashes, the AFE also reads the photodiode with all LEDs off to measure ambient light (sunlight, room lights) and subtract it. Pulsing at a known fast rate also lets it reject 50/60 Hz flicker.

2 · Clean. Split each colour into baseline (DC, via low-pass) and ripple (AC, via band-pass, roughly 0.5–8 Hz). Then fight motion — the number-one corruptor — usually with an accelerometer feeding an adaptive filter, plus throwing out any beat that doesn't look like a clean pulse.

3 · Extract features. Per colour: AC/DC. Across colours: the ratios and ratio-of-ratios. Plus pulse shape (upstroke, dicrotic notch, decay) and often age/sex. These physics-informed features are what let a model trained on many people generalise to a new finger.

4 · Estimate. Feed those features to a calibrated model. Could be a simple polynomial fit, but modern systems use machine learning — gradient-boosted trees, stacking regressors, sometimes CNNs on the waveform shape — because the feature-to-hemoglobin map in scattering tissue is nonlinear.

Key honesty point for your own build: the Beer–Lambert physics gives you the form of the relationship, but never the constants. You must calibrate against real lab hemoglobin draws, on real people. There is no first-principles shortcut to the coefficients.

The skin-tone problem is physics, not negligence

Melanin sits in the epidermis and absorbs strongly at the shorter (visible/red) wavelengths. Two consequences: it steals photons before they reach the blood (lower signal on darker skin), and — worse — its absorption isn't perfectly separable from hemoglobin's with a limited set of colours. So a model trained mostly on lighter skin blames some of that pigment absorption on hemoglobin and reads high. Published data on Masimo's SpHb backs this up: overestimation that scales with pigmentation. The defence is unglamorous but real — train on a deliberately pigment-diverse population, and lean on the NIR colours where melanin is weakest.

95% limits of agreement · g/dL
Read it as: narrower = better. A lab analyser nails it. A standard continuous-wave optical device — no matter how clever the algorithm — drifts out to ±2–3 g/dL in the field, because the missing pathlength and scattering information simply isn't in the measurement. The only way to tighten it (bottom bar) is to measure pathlength — time-resolved or diffuse-reflectance optics — not assume it.

Why no algorithm fixes this

You cannot compute information you never captured. A continuous-wave intensity reading doesn't contain the subject's true pathlength or scattering, so no amount of ML can recover them. Hemodilution studies make it stark: noninvasive hemoglobin has good bias (the average is about right) but poor precision — only ~40–45% of the variation tracks the real lab value. The rest is the person's own optics. This is a precision problem baked into the medium, not a sensitivity problem you can train away.

If you're the one building it

A hardware engineer's checklist

  1. Anchor at 805 nm, then bracket it. Put colours on both sides (e.g. 660/735 below, 880/940 above) plus one visible band (~590 nm) for melanin sensing. Treat 940 nm as the ceiling — never past ~970 nm where water takes over.
  2. Spec the AFE for ambient rejection and dynamic range first. You want dark-phase subtraction, synchronous detection, per-LED current DACs, a 19–24-bit ADC, and an auto-gain loop targeting ~30–40% of full scale. With a 1% signal, front-end SNR is the gate on everything.
  3. Build physics-informed features, then fit empirically. AC/DC per colour, cross-wavelength ratios, the isosbestic total-Hb feature → feed a gradient-boosted-tree or stacking model. Always cross-validate by subject, never by beat, or you'll fool yourself with leakage.
  4. Calibrate against a real lab reference on a pigment- and Hb-diverse cohort. Span at least 5–20 g/dL and the full skin-tone range. Training-set diversity is your single best defence against pigment bias — more than any clever optics.
  5. Gate every output on signal quality. Show a confidence flag, not a falsely precise 12.3. A wrong number with a decimal point is worse than an honest "low signal."
When to change strategy

If validated error exceeds ~1.5 g/dL (or limits of agreement exceed ±2.5–3): you have a screening/trend device, not a transfusion-decision device. Position it that way and require a lab draw near the 7–8 g/dL decision zone.

If you genuinely need lab-grade absolute accuracy (<1 g/dL): continuous-wave PPG won't get there. Escalate to time-resolved, frequency-domain, or diffuse-reflectance optics that actually measure pathlength.

Keeping me honest

What's solid vs. what's representative

Rock solid: the Beer–Lambert framework, the isosbestic point, the AC/DC and ratio-of-ratios maths, and the published hemoglobin absorption spectra. These are textbook.

Representative, not gospel: the exact 590/660/735/805/880/940 nm set, the per-channel weightings, AFE duty cycles, filter corners, and model choices vary by implementation — and commercial vendors (Masimo especially) don't publish their precise wavelength sets, calibration coefficients, or ML architectures. Treat those numbers as well-motivated defaults, not vendor-confirmed specs.

The absorption curve in the hero is plotted through the six tabulated extinction values from the source document; the smooth line between those points is an illustrative guide, not measured data. Values at 735 and 805 nm are interpolated from a 2-nm reference grid, and 590 nm sits near — not exactly at — the visible isosbestic (the true crossing is closer to ~584–586 nm). The ±2–3 g/dL real-world figure is a synthesis of several independent studies, not one authoritative number.
